LFA Programming Rules
If any section is unclear or conflicts with another section, use Section 17 Addendum as the final authority.
0. Core LFA principles
LFA is a strength and conditioning app for local footy athletes. It is not a medical advice app, diagnosis tool, or rehab program.
The app’s job is to build and adjust S&C programming around the athlete’s real life: their season phase, game day, team training, goals, schedule, injuries, soreness, illness, equipment access, missed sessions, and readiness.
The north star is simple:
Do the optimal amount of work.
Enough to improve or maintain performance.
Not so much that the athlete burns out, breaks down, or feels cooked all the time.
Not so little that they lose strength, fitness, or momentum.
LFA should always try to get as close as possible to my ideal programming model, while adapting to the athlete’s actual week.
If an athlete is injured, sick, sore, busy, or missing equipment, the app should not simply stop everything. It should find what they can still do.
Example:
Shoulder issue:
Avoid affected pressing or upper-body work.
Keep lower-body strength, bike conditioning, core, mobility, and other safe work where appropriate.
The goal is to keep athletes training intelligently around problems, not to make them fragile or afraid to train.
At the same time, if something is too severe, complex, or outside normal S&C adjustment, LFA should stop affected training and tell the athlete to seek medical or physio advice. The app should not pretend to diagnose or rehab injuries.
The user is ultimately in control. They can override the system and add extra work if they want. The app should allow this, but it should give a clear warning when the user is likely doing too much.
Example:
You can add this session, but that gives you 4 hard sessions this week. Make sure you are recovering well.
The same works the other way. If the athlete is consistently doing too little, skipping sessions, or not getting enough useful work in, the app should be able to gently nudge them.
Example:
You have only completed 1 session this week. Try to get one more quality session in if you can.
The programming should stay simple and practical. The same key ideas run through the whole year: movement patterns, strength, conditioning, sprint exposure, recovery, and availability.
What changes across the year is the emphasis.
Off-season:
More room to build strength, size, fitness, weak points, and capacity.
Pre-season:
More running, conditioning, sprint exposure, and game-readiness while managing team training load. But still building strength and size. 
In-season:
Maintain strength and fitness, protect freshness for game day, manage soreness and injuries, and keep the athlete available.
Position matters, but it should not dominate the program.
Most local footy athletes need the same big rocks:
Get stronger.
Get fitter.
Sprint regularly enough to stay exposed.
Recover well.
Stay available.
Build around games and team training.
Position can slightly bias the program, but the bigger drivers should be:
Season phase
Athlete goals
Training age
Fitness level
Strength level
Injury status
Recent training load
Schedule
Availability
For example, a ruck and a small forward may have some different priorities, but both still need strength, conditioning, sprint exposure, robustness, and recovery. The app should not overcomplicate position-based programming unless there is a clear reason.
The app should feel like a smart S&C coach in the background:
The athlete taps what is happening.
The app adjusts the program.
Coach Notes explain what changed and why.
The athlete can clear or update restrictions when things change.
The visible AI chat should not be the main way program changes happen. The AI chat can recommend ideas, but routine changes should be handled through simple tap/edit functions built into the app. The chat bot is not the main product.
LFA is not about becoming a professional athlete. It is about helping local footballers get the most out of themselves and build some of the best memories of their lives.
1. Season phase rules
In-season

* Main goal: maintain strength, fitness, work around injuries, deliver the optimal amount of S&C week i.e. get as much in as possible without compromising performance or risking injury and burnout. 
* Weekly priorities: be fresh for game day. lift 2x minimum per week. train well at footy training which means not doing work that will compromise TT. 
* What matters most near game day: freshness for the athlete. Most athletes will train G-2. So G-1 should be a rest or optional gunshow. Some athletes will have a light TT the day before a game. That's fine. But we should not program hard conditioning or heavy lowers g-1 or even g-2. 
* What not to overdo: hard conditioning outside of TT unless user is only doing 1 TT per week. Sprint work in season - if user is completely TT 2 times per week then sprint training is unnecessary. Over stimulating certain muscle groups i.e. 6 hamstring or hinge lifts per week in season would be too many. 
* Strength goal: Maintain or improve upper body lifts. Maintain lower body lifts as much as possible. 
* Conditioning goal: Maintenance. 
* Sprint/COD goal: Maintenance. No extra sprint work in season for athletes is a pretty good rule of thumb. 
* Recovery goal: Be fresh for game day. An athlete should not constantly be saying they are sick tired or injured. 
* How to handle soreness: depends on severity. Light flushouts can actually be helpful. Same as simple walking or recovery days. Don't add extra running if sore. Also depends on site of soreness. Good rule of thumb for injuries and soreness is work around it or attack that area lightly. Blood slow and movement is generally good for small niggles or soreness 
* How to handle injuries: depends on severity. Anything above a 5/10 should be looked at more carefully. this will be handled in injury section below. Work around the injury as much as possible. Continue to do work on unaffected areas. Then lighten loads and or volume for injured areas. If severe then stop targeting that area at all and let them get physio advice. 
* How to handle missed sessions: User is made aware of missed sessions in the app and can then choose if they did it, missed it, skipped it etc. skipped sessions are fine. If it happens every time then maybe program adjustments need to be made. but life gets in the way and people are going to be more or less diligent with logging their sessions. 
* How to handle busy weeks: see what they can do, see what they know they're going to miss, if they schedule missed sessions early enough, the program may be able to adapt to get in majority of the missed work at another time during the week. But it's okay to just have a busy week and go back to normal the next week. 
* How to handle bye weeks: If user is feeling good then training load can increase - not too much running (keep to 4 or less per week) but sometimes byes are a good time to get in extra work and strength training - especially for lower body work. On the Saturday, or usual game day, a hard conditioning session is usually a good idea. Again, this depends on user readiness. Byes can be a good time to reset and rest and prepare for future weeks. 
* How to handle moving game day: sometimes this will allows an extra session into the week but then effect the following week - there should be no "core" or compulsory sessions the day before or after a game - for example game usually saturday, thursday might be team training and upper body push, friday gunshow, saturday game, sunday rest or recovery. If that get's moved to sunday then monday becomes rest and recovery - whatever was on that monday is either skipped or spread throughout the rest of the next week, and saturday becomes gunshow. Which means friday is free to do maybe accessories and a flushout off legs or soemthing like that. 
* What should never be placed 1-2 days before a game: hard conditioning (not including team training as this is oftern G-2), heavy lowers 
* What is acceptable 1-2 days before a game: upper body training, gunshow, accessories, low volume low range power work like high box squats for low reps and vertical jumps, any recovery stuff or mobility flows are fine. 
* What should happen the day after a game: rest or recovery day - user can always add a session i.e. a flushout or aerobic base session if they want to (some athletes like doing this) but maybe just put a warning
* Ideal weekly structure: 3 options. Assuming saturday game and 2 team trainings on tuesday and thursday. Option 1 = monday lower body strength and optional flushout/ aerobic conditioning off-leg, tuesday upper body pull plus team training, wednesday rest or optional flushout/ aerobic conditioning off-leg, thursday upper body push and team training, friday gunshow or recovery, saturday game, sunday rest or recovery. 2nd option, lower body squat + optional flushout/ aerobic conditioning off-leg, tuesday upper body pull + team training, wednesday lower body hinge + optional flushout/ aerobic conditioning off-leg, thursday upper body push + team training, friday gunshow or recovery, saturday game, sunday rest or recovery. 3rd option. monday Full body strength + optional flushout/ aerobic conditioning off-leg, tuesday team training, wednesday Full body strength + optional flushout/ aerobic conditioning off-leg, thursday team training, friday gunshow or recovery, saturday game, sunday rest or recovery.
Pre-season

* Main goal: build strength, fitness, and be fresh for team training sessions. Prepare for in season mode and games - so injury resilience is very important to build here. Avoiding soft tissue injuries is never 100% possible but risk can be reduced. Good time to overreach a little bit. But don't risk injury at training. 
* Weekly priorities: strength, conditioning, injury resilience, team training. 
* Running/conditioning goal: improve. 
* Strength goal: improve. A healthy no-game week with normal availability requires 4 main-strength exposures in early, mid and late pre-season. Safety or feasibility may reduce this only through an explicit typed authorised reason.
* Sprint exposure goal: at least 1 genuine sprint/high-speed exposure per week. Team training and practice matches receive sprint/high-speed anchor credit, and no extra app sprint is required when those anchors already satisfy the floor. A second exposure may occur naturally, but it is not the shared required minimum.
* Team training effect: most clubs will train for 90 mins or so and do 7-8km of running, pre season sessions are generally harder on the body than in season, especially if training hard outside of team training too. Most clubs will train 2-3 times per week in pre season. 
* How to avoid overload: make sure core or compulsory sessions are limited to 4 conditioning and 4 strength. This includes team training as part of that 4. Optional lighter sessions or flushouts can be done to. Running limited to 4 times per week. Sprinting limited to 2 nights per week which includes nights at team training. 
* How fast should load progress: no faster than 10% per week. Unless very beginner to training. 
* What not to overdo: sprint work outside of footy training. more than 2 lowers body sessions per week. 
* How to handle soreness/injury: same as anything, if you can work around it do so, if it's severe then seek medical or phsyio advice. Get as much work as you can in around the injury. 
* Ideal weekly structure: training is generally monday wednesday and sometimes fridat - occasionally teams will train 4 times in pre season but that's very rare. Healthy no-game early, mid and late pre-season all require 4 main-strength and 4 total conditioning exposures. Team training counts toward the conditioning total, so 2 team trainings normally leave 2 app conditioning components. Generally upper lower upper lower 2 team trainings and 2 extra conditioning plus optional 5th cardio. You can double up upper body sessions with team training. Other weeks may be more like 3 x full body and 2 team trainings with 1-2 extra conditioning. Some people may be more like lower body, upper body and full body, and 2 team sessions plus 1-2 extra conditioning sessions. if can only do 2 strength sessions should be 2 x full body and those sessions should be pretty solid. You can have 1 hard conditioning session outside of TT if TT is 2x per week. and then 1-2 lighter sessions like a flushout or aerobic base. you don't need to add app sprint work if team training already supplies the weekly sprint/high-speed floor.
* Early pre-season vs late pre-season: early pre season is more base building, longer sessions, slightly lighter intensity, then as the season moves closer to end of pre season = shorten sessions, reduce volume, but increase intensity. Phase progression changes prescription, dose, intensity and specificity; it does not delete the healthy 4-strength / 4-conditioning baseline frequency.
* Deload rules: once every 4 weeks or so deload. This can be just changing the program and starting with a lighter week in the new program. I've always liked 3-4 weekly cycles. Deload when user is fatigued or showing a lack of readiness too. 
Off-season

* Main goal: build size, strength and muscle. Especially if their goal is to build size. Recover any injuries form the year prior. 
* Weekly priorities: strength and size is main focus early in off season, then we focus on building capacity through higher volume lower intensity conditioning
* Strength/hypertrophy goal: main focus in general. Unless otherwise stated by the user. 
* Running/conditioning goal: build slowly over off season - by the last few weeks of off season, should be going pretty hard at conditioning. If they have said their main goal is fitness, and running out games etc then the focus should be on that instead of strength and size. 
* Sprint exposure: early off-season has no required sprint exposure. From mid off-season onward, require at least 1 genuine sprint/high-speed exposure per week unless an explicit typed safety or feasibility reduction applies.
* Mobility/prehab: important, off season is avery good time to make gains in mobility that may have limited them during the season. Trying to increase mobility during the season or even in pre season when you have games and demands of changing direction, bending, twisting, body contact, awkward positions etc then you risk injury - so off season when it's mostly strength work and controlled conditionig = great time to focus on mobility - I will have some mobility flow sessions in the program 
* Weak point work: KEY thing in off season is working on weakness - may be mobility and injury prevention, or strength and size, or conditioning, or speed. What the user said is their weakness is the focus here. If they don't really say they have a weakness then strength and size should be prioritised early, then building work capacity, then increasing speed and intensity as we get closer to pre season. 
* How hard can the app push: very. no team training or games means conditioning is cotnrolled = no outside forces actign on the athlete. No risk of overdoing the gym work but then having to front up to team training the next night, and SURPISE, coach has a practice match planned = which is the equivalent of doing a really hard gym session on the lower body the day before a game, so in general - the app can push pretty hard in off season because everything is controlled. 
* What not to overdo: focusing on strengths too much. For example, an athlete is super fit but weak, not much point in smashing out running and fitness stuff. They should be focused on strength = off season is fix weakness season. 
* Ideal weekly structure: this really all depends on their goals and weaknesses. 3-4 strengths, 3-5 conditionioning and 1-2 mobility is probably a decent starting point. You should have an idea of ideal programming from my pre season and in season templates. For someone who needed general strength and fitness I would do lower body + conditoning, upper body + conditoning, rest wednesday, ower body + conditoning, upper body + conditoning, saturday long slow run, sunday rest = something like that is very good but it is general = again focus on their weaknesses. 
* Early off-season vs late off-season: early off season as soon as season finishes, the first two weeks should be optional sessions for everything, people should enjoy time off, early off season = no running and only light aerobic work / base building work. Later in off-season can start building running capacity. Early off season focused on just movign and feeling good, slightly more bodybuilderr style programming - sets of 8-12 to add some body armour. Then 6-8 reps as move toward end of off season. Pre season generally starts round november. Games finish in august/september. So generally have 2 months in off season, sometimes 3. 
* Deload rules: once every 4 weeks or so deload. This can be just changing the program and starting with a lighter week in the new program. I've always liked 3-4 weekly cycles. Deload when user is fatigued or showing a lack of readiness too. May need less deload here in terms of conditioning - but lifting will be causing some soreness so changing the stimulus and having lighter weeks is good. 
2. Weekly structure rules
This is where you define how the week should be built.

* Game day is treated as: Game day is the main anchor of the week. The whole week should be built around being fresh for game day. The app should avoid hard lower-body strength, hard conditioning, heavy sprint/COD work, or anything that could leave the athlete cooked too close to the game. Weeks without games can be used as a rest or a min preseason to go harder. The program should move based on game days moving. 
* Team training is treated as: Team training is treated as a real training session, not just skills. It counts as running, sprint exposure, change of direction, contact, and general fatigue. The app should avoid stacking too much hard lower-body or conditioning work on top of hard team training unless the user chooses to override it. Most team sessions will be 90 min long, 7-8km and involve sprinting, repeat sprint efforts, fast running, a lot of walking and jogging and skills work. 
* Rest days are: Rest days are days with no required S&C work. They are used to freshen up, absorb training, recover from games, or create space around heavy training days. Optional easy recovery is okay which involves a light mobility flow, and potentially a walk or soemthing, but rest days should not secretly become hard training days.
* How many hard days per week max: 4 = but this could be 4 hard days with doubles of srength + conditioning every day. This would be max in off season and then you could also add an easier aerobic session to the sunday. So really you could have 8-9 indivudally moderate to hard sessions per week in off season. and this would change at different points of the year i.e. in season gme + 2 team training = 3 + 3 hard strength = 6 sessions, even though that may be spread over 3-4 days. 
* What counts as a hard day: team training, game day, hard conditioning, interval work, strength days that aren't gun shows, accessories, recovery. 
* Can hard sessions stack? yes - it's fine to put strength and conditioning on the same day. I wouldnt stack lower body strength and upper body strength in the same day, would prefer full body session but the user could override that if their week required it for whatever reason. 
* Can strength and conditioning be on the same day? yes absolutely and I'd almost prefer it so give them 1-2 days fully off each week. For example, if I had to get 7 hard sessions in one week, Id prefer to do that over 5 days and give them 2 days fully off vs doing 1 hard session per day. 
* When is a double day okay? stacking Strength and conditioning is good. I wouldn't stack lower body strength (say 6-7 exercises) with upper body strength (6-7 exercises) because that would be 12-14 lifts in one day). I'd rpefer to just make that a full body day i.e. full body strength and 7 exercises. You can always add a recovery or mobility flow to any day as optional. 
* When is a double day not okay? only when there are very competing demands in eachs ession i.e. a lower body hinge day should NOT be paired with a sprint session at the oval = too much risk over overtraining or injury. when possible pair upper body strength with conditioning days, and lower body strength can be paired with conditioning but preferably off-leg conditioning like bike, ski, rower, assault bike. 
* How many lower-body exposures per week: ideally 2-3 but in season may only be 1. 
* How many upper-body exposures per week: 2-3 all year really
* How many conditioning exposures per week: 3-5 depending on phase of year, that me just be 2x team training and game day in season with optional extra off-leg conditioning, off season may be 5 sessions 
* How many sprint/COD exposures per week: the year-round required minimum is 1 genuine sprint/high-speed exposure per week except early off-season. Team training, games and practice matches receive anchor credit; do not add unnecessary app sprint work when those anchors satisfy the floor. A second exposure may occur naturally, while 2-3 remains the usual maximum. Any reduction below the floor requires an explicit typed authorised reason. Deloads should preserve the exposure through reduced volume/dose unless safety or feasibility explicitly reduces it.
* How many recovery sessions per week: as many as wanted or can fit. I'd say 1-2 
* Rules around G-2: no heavy lower body work or speed work (TT will often be 2 days before game)
* Rules around G-1: same as above, no heavy lfiting of any sort, no conditioning, only gunshow, accessories or recovery = all optional as well. 
* Rules around game day: no rules, just play, no programmed sessions
* Rules around G+1: complete rest or recovery
* Rules around team training: no real rules, prefer to not do lower body before training but athletes week may not allow for this. 
* Rules around bye week: train harder i.e. mini pre season like an off season week OR use as a really good time to rest and recover i.e. bigger deload week 
* Rules around missed sessions: doesn't matter too much. Different athletes wil be more or less diligent filling them in. Just give the pop up in the app "did you complete this session" - if continue missing sessions then program may need to change. 
* Rules around busy/away weeks: depends on what the athlete can do. they will say when they'r away and maybe make all sessions optional during that time. If busy then can move sessions if the week allows it or simply skip. 
3. Hard exposure rules
This needs its own section.
Define what counts as stress.
High stress - all these are high stress

* Game
* Team training hard
* Lower-body strength
* Sprint/COD
* Hard conditioning
* Heavy hinge/squat
* High volume running
Medium stress

* Upper strength - could be classed as high stress if easier
* Moderate conditioning - could be classed as high stress if easier
* Full body strength - could be classed as high stress if easier
* Prehab/accessory - could be classed as low stress if easier
* Tempo/aerobic - could be classed as low stress if easier
Low stress

* Recovery
* Mobility
* Walk/bike flush
* Upper pump
* Easy core
* Note on this - do not program ski erg rower or assault bike as a 25 min flush being an easy session - don't even program it at all - you can do a 30 min regular bike but for something like a row or ski 3x8 on 2 off or something is better - it is just uncomfortable on there to do a straight 30 min session on the rower and it is not something I would program - also feel free to make it like 8 min on 2 min off row, 8 min on 2 min off ski, 8 min on 2 min off bike as much session = i would do that quite often for a flush out session
Then write rules:

* Max high-stress exposures per week in-season: 2x team training, 1 game, then you can have 2-4 strength days (depending on preference and availability) and an optional conditioning plus gunshow if wanted
* Max high-stress exposures per week pre-season: 1-3 team training, 1-3 extra conditioning depending on team training, 2-4 strength - again double ups are good - see pre season details for that plus gunshow if wanted
* Max high-stress exposures per week off-season: up to 4 strengths pluys recovery mobility gunshow if wanted, 3-5 conditioning
* How close can high stress be to game day: well there is generally team training on G-2 (thursday for saturday game) so last high stress would be then along with upper body (medium stress really) - so last additional high stress is G-3
* How close can sprint work be to game day: don't do sprint work in season = get enough at training and during game 
* How close can lower strength be to game day: g-3 (g-2 if it's low range of motion, low reps, high quality i.e. 2x3 box squats to high box + 2x3 vertical jumps) - low volume, not many exercises 
* What to do if team training is already hard: then conditioning should be optional in season, but in pre season and off season, athlete should aim for as close to 4 hard/mediums per week with optional light cardio on top of that 
* What to do if athlete says they are cooked: make sessions optional for one week after, tell them it's a deload, if they want to train still then they can - but just encourage them to use lighter weights and we may need to adjust sets and reps for them
4. Session types
Main idea
Session type tells the app what the day is trying to achieve.
The exact prescription still depends on:
Season phase
Game day
Team training
Available days
Training age
Strength level
Fitness level
Injuries
Fatigue/readiness
Goals
The app should not treat every session as equal. A lower strength day, team training, hard conditioning day, recovery session and rest day all create different stress.
Lower strength
Purpose: Build or maintain lower-body strength, power, muscle, robustness and body armour for footy.
Lower strength should help the athlete handle:
Contact
Acceleration
Deceleration
Jumping
Landing
Kicking
Changing direction
Repeat efforts
Awkward game positions
Main movement patterns:
Squat
Hinge
Single-leg
Hamstring
Adductor/groin
Calf/Achilles
Core/carry
Squat: Build knee-dominant lower-body strength. Good options include back squat, front squat, box squat, goblet squat, safety bar squat, Bulgarian split squat if used as a main lift.
Hinge: Build posterior-chain strength. Good options include trap bar deadlift, deadlift, RDL, single-leg RDL, hip thrust, back extension.
Single-leg: Build unilateral strength, balance, hip control and lower-limb robustness. Good options include Bulgarian split squat, reverse lunge, step-up, split squat, walking lunge, single-leg RDL.
Hamstring: Build hamstring strength and capacity for sprinting, kicking and repeat efforts. Good options include Nordics, hamstring curls, RDLs, single-leg RDLs, hip thrusts, bridges, Bosch holds.
Adductor: Build groin/adductor capacity for sprinting, kicking, lateral movement and COD. Good options include groin squeeze, Copenhagens, adductor machine, lateral lunge.
Calf: Build lower-leg and tendon capacity for running, sprinting, jumping and repeat efforts. Good options include standing calf raise, seated calf raise, single-leg calf raise, tib raises.
Core: Build trunk stiffness, bracing and control. Good options include dead bug, Pallof press, side plank, carries, chops, hanging knee raises.
In-season version: Lower strength should be familiar, lower volume, high quality and not too close to game day.
Usually best early in the week.
For Saturday game:
Best: G-5 / G-4 / G-3
Avoid: G-2 unless tiny low-volume power exposure
Never: G-1 as a proper lower session
In-season lower strength is mainly about maintaining strength and keeping the body exposed to heavy work without creating soreness.
Pre-season version: Lower strength should build or maintain strength while respecting team training load.
Pre-season team training is already a major lower-body/running stress, so the app should avoid stacking hard lower strength right next to hard field sessions where possible.
Off-season version: More room to build strength, size, weak points and tissue capacity.
Off-season can use more volume, more hypertrophy work, more unilateral work, and more weak-point development.
Knee robustness / knee prehab rules
Purpose
Knee robustness work helps the athlete tolerate running, jumping, landing, deceleration, cutting, squatting and contact.
This is not rehab. It is S&C accessory work that can help build capacity around the knee.
Examples
Banded TKEs
Slant board step-downs
Step-down variations
Split squat variations
Reverse lunges
Spanish squat holds if available
Controlled single-leg work
Tempo step-ups
Leg extension if tolerated
When to use
Knee robustness work fits best as:
Lower-body accessory
Warm-up prep
Prehab/accessory session
Gunshow/prehab day
Return-to-normal controlled lower-body work
Sets / reps
Simple guide:
2-3 sets
8-15 reps
Controlled tempo
Low to moderate intensity
For isometric holds:
2-4 sets
20-45 seconds
Intensity
Usually:
4-7 / 10
Controlled
Smooth
No sharp pain
No grinding
The goal is control and tolerance, not max effort.
Phase rules
In-season
Use small doses only.
Good in-season knee work:
Banded TKEs 2 x 15
Slant board step-downs 2 x 8 each side
Controlled step-downs 2 x 8 each side
Do not create soreness close to game day.
Pre-season
Can build more knee capacity, especially if the athlete tolerates it well.
Off-season
Best time to build more knee strength, single-leg control and range.
What to avoid
Do not:
Force painful knee range
Turn knee prehab into a brutal leg session
Add high-volume step-downs close to game day
Use jump/plyo work when knee symptoms are active
Treat knee prehab as medical rehab
Injury considerations
If knee pain is active:
Use controlled range
Avoid painful depth
Avoid hard COD
Avoid jumping/landing
Avoid hard deceleration
Use bike/off-feet conditioning if running flares it
Anything 6/10 or above should recommend physio/medical advice and remove hard knee loading.

Purpose: Build or maintain upper-body strength, size, contact strength, shoulder robustness and body armour.
Upper strength is usually easier to place around game day than lower strength, but shoulder/elbow fatigue still matters.

Examples:
Face pulls

Arms: Useful for body armour, contact, confidence and gunshow-style low-fatigue work.
Examples:
Bicep curls

Good options:
Pallof press
Side plank
Dead bug
Carries

In-season version: Upper strength can stay in year-round. It can often be placed before team training or closer to game day than lower strength.
In-season upper should usually be:
Heavy enough to maintain strength
Not pushed to failure
Not excessive volume
Balanced push and pull
Pre-season version: Upper strength is useful because it can be trained hard without adding as much field-running stress as lower-body work.
It can often pair well with team training days if needed.
Off-season version: More room for strength, size and upper-body volume.
Good time to build pressing, pulling, arms, shoulders and body armour. 
Shoulder stability / overhead control rules
Purpose
Shoulder stability work helps the athlete tolerate pressing, overhead positions, contact, marking, tackling and upper-body strength work.
This is not rehab. It is S&C accessory work for shoulder robustness and control.
Examples
Bottoms-up KB press
Bottoms-up KB carry
Landmine press
Half-kneeling landmine press
Face pull
Incline Y raise
External rotation
Scap push-up
Rear delt fly
Controlled DB overhead press if tolerated
Bottoms-up KB press
Bottoms-up KB press is mainly an overhead control and shoulder stability exercise.
It should be light, controlled and high quality.
Simple guide:
2-3 sets
5-8 reps each side
Light load
Slow control
No grinding
It is not a max strength lift.
When to use
Shoulder stability work fits best:
Upper-body accessory
Gunshow/prehab
Warm-up/prep
Key position / ruck / tall bias
Shoulder robustness work
It can be useful for taller/key position players because they often need more overhead control for marking, contact and contested aerial work.
Phase rules
In-season
Use low volume and familiar variations.
Do not create shoulder soreness close to game day.
Pre-season
Can build more shoulder capacity and overhead control.
Off-season
Best time to build shoulder robustness, pressing tolerance and overhead control.
What to avoid
Do not:
Force overhead work if shoulder pain is active
Use bottoms-up KB press as a heavy lift
Program unstable shoulder work to fatigue
Use hard overhead work close to game day if shoulders are sore
Swap painful overhead press for another painful overhead press
Injury considerations
If shoulder pain is active:
Reduce painful pressing
Avoid heavy overhead work
Avoid dips if painful

Anything 6/10 or above should recommend physio/medical advice and avoid painful pressing/overhead/contact work.

* Hard conditioning hidden inside the same day without counting the load. 
In-season version: Lower volume. Familiar exercises. Moderate total dose. Good option when the athlete can only lift once or twice.
Pre-season version: Useful as a support strength session around team training. Should not compete with hard field work.

When rest is required:

* When bedridden or properly sick. 
* When fatigue is high. 
* When injury severity requires affected training to pause. 
* When the week is already full. 
* When adding a session would create a bad stack. 
* When the athlete has a busy/away week and cannot train.

Light tissue quality

5. Strength programming rules
This section should be very detailed.
Main lift rules

* Main lift purpose: Build strength and power in basic movement pattern, do the basics ruthlessly well. If you can get a strong bench, squat, RDL, pull up, row and overhead press - you're going to be very well rounded and have a good amount of body armour. Using these same lifts all year round we can minimise the chance of random soreness in season because your body is used to lifting this way. Which means we can continue piling on the weights getting stronger and more powerful all yeah and maintaining muscle mass. 
I generally break it down into hinge, squat, push (overhead and horizontal), pull (overhead and horizontal), knee dominant leg, hip dominant single leg, arms, lower body accessories, core, carry. Then those categories can be broken down further if needed like single arm, rotational core, anti-flexion, anti-extension and so on. 
* Sets/reps in-season: 2-4 sets of 2-4 reps in season. 3x3 is generally pretty good. Especially for lower body. Upper body could be closer to that 3x5. And even 3x6 for pulling exercises - I find your pulling muscles can generally tolerate higher loads. Soreness should not be too much of an issue in season because we have already learned all these moves in off season so doing split squats or nordics will not be surprise soreness your body is not used to especailly if you keep the reps low, high quality and volume not too high
* Sets/reps pre-season: This can be slightly more than in season. I like 2-4 sets of 4-6 reps here, 3x5 is generally good for strength and muscle building at the same time. Anything more than 6 becomes more focused on muscle building anything less than 5 is more about strength and less muscle building and you want a combination here. Same principles apply with the push vs pull stuff 
* Sets/reps off-season: Slightly more than pre season. 2-4 sets of 6-8 reps here is good. 3 x 8 is a good base. Some exercises can go up to 12 reps especially for things like pull ups, lat pull downs and row variations. But again, if something is strong, and heavy and says they want to work on fitness mostly, I'd keep the muscle building to a minimum more focused on the 2-4 range. If there goal is muscle mass it should be more in the realm of 6-10 reps. 3 sets is a good starting point for most things. 
* Intensity/RPE: Depends on season phase, in season should be big weights, not to complete fatigue or max though. Could say 7/8 out of 10. Pre season off season with slightly higher reps they can go closer to failure and grind a little bit more in the 8/9 out of 10 range.
* Rest: I don't really prescribe rest. If i said anything about rest it would be rest until you feel ready to do the next set. On main lifts, you should not be breathing heavy going into a lift. 
* When to go heavy: year round, don't go as close to failure in season. I have no problem with an athlete doing heavy lowers on a monday and sometimes even a wednesday if they have been training year round. Just keep the reps high quality, not too close to failure, and limiting range of motion for something like a box squat can help. 
* When not to go heavy: when injured, when sick, when super tired, when close to a game (for lower body lifts)
* Progression rule: small jumps each week if they want to and are new and feeling good. But not too big a jump each week. Also, maybe once every new program for stronger guys i.e. 3 weeks of 3x3 at 100kg bench, next block may be 3x3 at 102.5kg or even slightly different could be like 3x5 incline bench press at 75kg - so it's the same kind of stimulus but slight variation. 
* Deload rule: new program every 3-4 weeks. can change the lift variation as mentioned above so it still fits the push or pull, squat or hinge and so on patterns but it's a variation. 
Squat anchors: Back Squat, Front Squat, Box Squat, Bulgarian Split Squats,
Hinge anchors: Deadlift, Trap Bar Deadlift, RDLs,  Single-Leg RDL, 
Horizontal push: Bench Press, Incline Bench, Close Grip Bench, DB Bench Press, Incline DB Bench, Push-ups, Dips.
Vertical push: Overhead Press, Landmine Press, DB Shoulder Press, Seated DB Press, Half-Kneeling Single-Arm Overhead Press, Z-Press.
Horizontal pull: Barbell Row, Chest Supported Row, Single-Arm DB Row, Seated Cable Row, Face Pull, Rear Delt Fly.
Vertical pull: Pull-Ups, Chin-Ups, Lat Pulldown, Single arm pulldown, Neutral-Grip Pulldown.
Strength balance rules
LFA should make best efforts to balance the major strength patterns across the week and across each training block.
This means balancing:

* upper and lower strength
* squat / knee-dominant work
* hinge / hip-dominant work
* horizontal push
* vertical push
* horizontal pull
* vertical pull
* single-leg work
* trunk / carry work
Gunshow, accessories, prehab, mobility and recovery work are useful, but they do not replace proper upper or lower strength exposure.
The app should not treat all strength sessions as interchangeable.
A lower strength session creates different stress and adaptation than an upper strength session. A squat/knee-dominant lower session creates a different stimulus than a hinge/hip-dominant lower session. A push session should be balanced with enough pulling over time.
Best-effort rule: If the athlete is healthy, available and recovering well, the app should try to keep the main strength patterns balanced.
This does not mean every week must be perfectly balanced. Game day, team training, injuries, fatigue, soreness, equipment and availability can all temporarily change the balance.
Examples:

* If lower strength is unsafe close to game day, preserve upper strength where possible.
* If pressing is restricted by a shoulder issue, pulling, lower body, core and shoulder-friendly work may dominate temporarily.
* If hamstring or lower-back issues restrict hinging, squat/knee-dominant work, upper body and safe core work may dominate temporarily.
* If the athlete only has one gym day, full body may be the best option.
Simple rule: Protect the game first, but do not make the program softer than it needs to be.
When a full lower or full-body session cannot fit safely, the app should consider:

* moving it earlier
* changing it to upper strength
* changing it to full body earlier in the week
* using gunshow/prehab only if proper strength genuinely cannot fit
* reducing the week only when necessary
Secondary lift rules

* Purpose: aid the main lifts in the gym and provide variation to the athlete so they don't get bored. If they main goal is to get stronger and more powerful in the main lifts, then the purpose of these is to fill in the gaps, help develop weak areas for the main lifts (i.e. developing the triceps to help with a stronger bench), reduce the risk of injury through prehab and even accessory lifts, 
* Sets/reps: usuaully go slightly higher reps here 2-3 sets of 10-20 reps works well on most things, some exercises like carries and planks would be timed i.e. 2-3 sets of 30-60 seconds, nordics lowers and the main one that should be less i.e. 3-5 reps max
* Intensity: 6 or 7/10 most of the time. Sometimes it can be good to do arms to failure i.e. 2 sets of max reps is something I frequently do with arms. 
* Progression: no real progressions here, just pick a weight that feels good, get a pump with it and then move on
* Examples:
Squat accessories: Walking Lunges, Reverse Lunges, Step Ups.
Hinge accessories: Hip Thrusts, Kettlebell Swings, back extensions
Lower accessories include Nordic Lower, Hamstring curl, Leg Extension, Calf Raises, Tib Raise, Adductor Machine.
Gunshow: biceps x2, triceps x2, delts x1, upper-back pump x1.
* Prehab/accessories: trunk anti-rotation, groin adductors, shoulder health, calves, hamstring-light, bosch hold
* Pool examples: Bicep Curl, Incline DB curl, Hammer Curl, Tricep Pushdown, Skull Crushers, Lateral Raise, Incline Y Raise, Face Pull, Nordic Lower, Calf Raises, Adductor Machine, Crab walks, copenhagen planks, groin squeeze, 
* Core/carries: Dead bug, Pallof press, Side plank, Farmer carry, Suitcase carry, bear carry, cable / chop variations, hanging knee/leg raises, dragon flags, ab wheel, 
Lower squat rules
Purpose: Build lower-body strength, power, knee-dominant strength, muscle, and body armour for footy. Squat patterns help athletes handle contact, acceleration, deceleration, jumping, landing, and general lower-body robustness.
Examples: Back squat, front squat, box squat, goblet squat, safety bar squat, Bulgarian split squat if used as a main lift.
Key categories:

* Bilateral squat: Back squat, front squat, box squat.
* Knee-dominant single-leg: Bulgarian split squat, reverse lunge, step-up.
* Controlled range squat: Box squat, goblet squat, tempo squat.
Main lift version: If the squat pattern is the main lift, follow the main lift rules.

* In-season: 2-4 sets of 2-4 reps. 3x3 is a good base. 
* Pre-season: 2-4 sets of 4-6 reps. 3x5 is a good base. 
* Off-season: 2-4 sets of 6-8 reps. 3x8 is a good base. 
Special rules:

* Squat work can stay in year-round if the athlete is used to it. 
* In-season squat work should be heavy enough to maintain strength, but not close to failure. 
* Avoid high-rep squat work in-season if it creates soreness. 
* Box squats or controlled range variations can be useful in-season to reduce soreness and keep quality high. 
* Do not place hard lower squat work too close to game day. 
* If knee, hip, groin, ankle or lower-back issues are active, reduce, swap or limit the squat pattern that aggravates it. 
* Pre-season and off-season can use more squat volume for strength, muscle and robustness. 
Lower hinge rules
Purpose: Build posterior-chain strength, hamstring capacity, hip strength, power, and body armour for sprinting, acceleration, deceleration, contact and repeat efforts.
Examples: Deadlift, trap bar deadlift, RDL, single-leg RDL, hip thrust, back extension, kettlebell swing.
Key categories:

* Heavy hinge: Deadlift, trap bar deadlift.
* Controlled hinge: RDL, single-leg RDL.
* Hip extension accessory: Hip thrust, back extension, kettlebell swing.
* Hamstring-biased hinge: RDL, single-leg RDL, Nordic lower depending context.
Main lift version: If the hinge pattern is the main lift, follow the main lift rules.

* In-season: 2-4 sets of 2-4 reps. 3x3 is a good base. 
* Pre-season: 2-4 sets of 4-6 reps. 3x5 is a good base. 
* Off-season: 2-4 sets of 6-8 reps. 3x8 is a good base. 
Special rules:

* Hinge work is important for hamstrings, hips, sprinting and general footy robustness. 
* Heavy hinge work can create fatigue and soreness, so be careful with placement in-season. 
* RDLs and single-leg RDLs are useful year-round, but volume should be controlled near games. 
* Avoid hard hinge work too close to game day, especially if the athlete has a history of hamstring or lower-back issues. 
* If hamstring or lower-back issues are active, reduce heavy hinges, RDLs, deadlifts and aggressive hamstring loading.
* Pre-season and off-season can use more hinge volume to build strength and posterior-chain capacity. 
* In-season hinge work should be familiar, high-quality, lower volume and not pushed to failure. 
Single-leg rules

* Purpose: Build unilateral strength, balance, hip control, knee/ankle stability and footy-specific robustness. Single-leg work helps athletes handle sprinting, cutting, landing, contact, deceleration and awkward game positions.
Examples: Bulgarian split squat, reverse lunge, step-up, walking lunge, split squat, single-leg RDL, lateral lunge.
Key categories:
* Knee-dominant single-leg: Bulgarian split squat, reverse lunge, walking lunge, step-up.
* Hip-dominant single-leg: Single-leg RDL, hip airplane style work, single-leg hip thrust.
* Lateral single-leg: Lateral lunge, Cossack squat, lateral step-down.
Main lift version: If single-leg work is being used as a main lift, follow the main lift rules.

1. 

* In-season: 2-4 sets of 2-4 reps each side. 3x3 is a good base.
* Pre-season: 2-4 sets of 4-6 reps each side. 3x5 is a good base.
* Off-season: 2-4 sets of 6-8 reps each side. 3x8 is a good base.
Accessory version: If single-leg work is being used as a secondary/accessory lift, it can be slightly higher rep.

1. 

* Usually 2-3 sets of 8-12 each side. 
* Only push higher if soreness is not an issue and it fits the phase. 
* In-season accessory single-leg volume should still be controlled. 
Special rules:

1. 

* Single-leg work can create a lot of soreness, especially split squats, lunges and step-ups. 
* In-season single-leg work should be familiar, low-volume, high-quality and not too close to game day. 
* Do not suddenly introduce hard Bulgarian split squats or high-volume lunges in-season if the athlete has not been doing them. 
* Pre-season and off-season can use more single-leg volume to build strength, muscle and robustness. 
* If knee, groin, hamstring, hip or ankle issues are active, reduce or swap the single-leg work that aggravates it. 
* Keep safe single-leg work in where possible, but do not force it if it causes pain or excessive soreness.
Hamstring rules

* Purpose: Build hamstring strength and capacity for sprinting, acceleration, deceleration, kicking, repeat efforts, and protecting the athlete from footy-related hamstring issues.
Examples: Nordic lowers, hamstring curls, RDLs, single-leg RDLs, hip thrusts, bridges, Bosch holds
Key categories:
* Knee flexion: Nordics, hamstring curls
* Hip extension: RDLs, single-leg RDLs, hip thrusts,
* Isometrics: Bridges, Bosch holds
Special rules:

1. 

* Hamstrings need both knee-flexion and hip-extension work. 
* Nordics should usually stay low rep because they can create a lot of soreness. 
* Hard hamstring work should not be placed too close to game day. 
* In-season hamstring work should be lower volume and familiar to the athlete. 
* Off-season and pre-season can build more hamstring capacity. 
* If a hamstring issue is active, reduce sprinting, max velocity work, aggressive running, heavy hinges, Nordics, and high-volume hamstring loading. 
* Keep safe work in where possible, such as upper body, core, easy bike, and non-painful lower-body work. 
Simple sets/reps guide:

1. 

* Nordics: 2-3 sets of 3-6 reps. 
* Hamstring curls: 2-3 sets of 8-15. 
* RDLs / single-leg RDLs: 2-4 sets of 5-10. 
* Bridges / isometrics: 2-4 sets of 20-45 sec.
Adductor/groin rules

* Purpose: Build groin/adductor strength and capacity for sprinting, kicking, change of direction, deceleration, lateral movement, and contact.
Examples: Groin squeeze, short-lever Copenhagen, long-lever Copenhagen, adductor machine, lateral lunge, Cossack squat.
Special rules:
* Important for footy because groins get loaded during sprinting, kicking, cutting and lateral movement. 
* In-season should mostly be maintenance, not chasing big new groin strength or mobility gains. 
* Avoid suddenly adding hard Copenhagens, deep lateral lunges, or aggressive groin stretching in-season if the athlete is not used to them. 
* More volume and harder variations are better suited to off-season and pre-season. 
* If groin pain is active, reduce sprinting, kicking, hard change of direction, deep lateral lunges and heavy adductor loading. 
* If symptoms are high, pause affected work and recommend physio/medical advice. 
* Keep safe work in where possible, such as upper body, core, easy bike, and non-painful lower-body work.
Calf/Achilles rules

* Purpose: Build lower-leg strength and tendon capacity for sprinting, jumping, landing, change of direction, repeated running, and staying durable through footy.
Examples: Standing calf raise, seated calf raise, single-leg calf raise, pogo jumps, skipping, sled pushes, tib raises.
Special rules:
* Straight-knee calf work targets more of the gastrocnemius. 
* Bent-knee calf work targets more of the soleus, which is important for running repeat efforts. 
* Calves and Achilles need regular exposure, but too much too fast can flare them up. 
* In-season should usually be small maintenance doses, not sudden high-volume calf work. 
* Plyometric prep can include pogos, skips, low hops, and landing work, but only if tolerated. used in warm up occasionally but would rarely program, may come back to in future
* If calf/Achilles pain is active, reduce sprinting, jumping, hard running, hill sprints, and high-volume calf raises. 
* Keep safe work in where possible, such as upper body, core, bike conditioning, and non-painful lower-body work. 
Simple sets/reps guide:

1. 

* Calf raises: 2 sets of 8-15. 
* Seated/bent-knee calf raises: 2 sets of 10-20. 
* Pogos/skips: small doses only, especially in-season. may be used in a warm up in the future, otherwise would never program 
Core/trunk rules

* Purpose: Build trunk strength, bracing, control and body armour for footy. Core work should help the athlete transfer force, stay strong in contact, sprint, change direction, land, twist, brace and hold good positions under fatigue.
Key categories:
Anti-extension: Stops the athlete from overextending through the lower back.
Examples: Dead bug, ab wheel, plank, stir the pot, hollow hold.
Anti-rotation: Builds control against twisting forces.
Examples: Pallof press, cable chops, cable lifts, landmine rotations, dead bug variations.
Lateral / anti-lateral flexion: Builds side-body strength and control.
Examples: Side plank, suitcase carry, side plank row.
Flexion: Direct ab work, usually higher control and not the main priority.
Examples: Hanging knee raises, hanging leg raises, dragon flags
Carries: Build trunk stiffness, grip, shoulder stability, posture and contact robustness.
Examples: Farmer carry, suitcase carry, bear carry
Sets/reps guide: Core and carries are mostly secondary/accessory work.
* Timed holds: 2-3 sets of 30-60 seconds. 
* Carries: 2-4 sets of 20-60 metres or 30-60 seconds. 
* Rep-based core: 2-3 sets of 8-15 reps. 
* Harder movements like ab wheel, dragon flags or heavy carries should stay lower volume. 
* Arms/core/accessory style work does not need strict progression. Pick a level that feels useful, controlled and repeatable. 
Special rules:

1. 

* Core should be included regularly, but it should not destroy the athlete. 
* In-season core should be familiar, low-soreness and not too fatiguing. 
* Pre-season and off-season can use more volume or harder carry variations. 
* Carries are useful for footy because they build bracing, grip, posture, trunk stiffness and general robustness. 
* If lower back, hip, groin, rib or shoulder issues are active, choose core variations that do not aggravate them. 
* Avoid turning core into a massive fatigue session when the athlete already has hard lifting, team training or a game coming up.
Upper push rules
Purpose: Build upper-body pushing strength, chest/shoulder/triceps strength, contact strength, body armour, and general upper-body power for footy.
Examples: Bench press, incline bench, close-grip bench, DB bench press, incline DB bench, push-ups, dips, overhead press, landmine press, DB shoulder press, half-kneeling single-arm OHP, Z-press.
Key categories:

* Horizontal push: Bench press, incline bench, close-grip bench, DB bench, push-ups, dips.
* Vertical push: Overhead press, DB shoulder press, landmine press, half-kneeling OHP, Z-press.
* Shoulder-friendly push: Landmine press, neutral-grip DB press, push-ups, floor press.
* Triceps/accessory push: Close-grip bench, dips, tricep pushdowns, skull crushers.
Main lift version: If upper push is being used as a main lift, follow the main lift rules.

* In-season: usually 2-4 sets of 3-5 reps. 3x5 is a good base.
* Pre-season: 2-4 sets of 4-6 reps. 3x6 is a good base.
* Off-season: 2-4 sets of 6-8 reps. If the goal is more muscle, push work can sit more around 6-10 reps.
Accessory version: If upper push is being used as secondary/accessory work:

* Usually 2-3 sets of 8-15 reps. 
* Triceps and pump work can go higher, around 10-20 reps. 
* Arms can sometimes go close to failure, especially in Gunshow-style sessions. 
Special rules:

* Upper push can stay in year-round if the athlete is used to it. 
* In-season pressing should be heavy enough to maintain strength, but not pushed to failure. 
* Pressing usually creates less game-day soreness than hard lower-body work, but shoulder/elbow irritation still matters. 
* Keep upper push balanced with enough pulling. 
* Avoid too much overhead pressing if shoulder, neck, rib, or back issues are active. 
* If shoulder pain is active, swap heavy bench, dips, or overhead work for safer options like landmine press, neutral-grip DB press, push-ups, or floor press. 
* Do not force pressing variations that cause pain. Keep safe upper or lower work in where possible. 
Upper pull rules
Purpose: Build back, lat, scapular, grip, and upper-body pulling strength. Upper pull supports tackling, marking, contact, posture, shoulder health, and balances out all pressing work.
Examples: Pull-ups, chin-ups, lat pulldown, neutral-grip pulldown, single-arm pulldown, barbell row, chest-supported row, single-arm DB row, seated cable row, face pull, rear delt fly.
Key categories:

* Vertical pull: Pull-ups, chin-ups, lat pulldown, neutral-grip pulldown, single-arm pulldown.
* Horizontal pull: Barbell row, chest-supported row, single-arm DB row, seated cable row.
* Scap/rear delt: Face pull, rear delt fly, incline Y raise.
* Grip/body armour: Pull-ups, rows, carries.
Main lift version: If upper pull is being used as a main lift, follow the main lift rules, but pulling can usually tolerate slightly higher reps than pressing.

* In-season: usually 2-4 sets of 4-6 reps. 3x5 is a good base.
* Pre-season: 2-4 sets of 4-8 reps.
* Off-season: 2-4 sets of 6-12 reps, especially for pull-ups, pulldowns and row variations.
Accessory version: If upper pull is secondary/accessory work:

* Usually 2-3 sets of 8-15 reps. 
* Rear delt, face pull and upper-back pump work can sit around 12-20 reps. 
* Pulling accessories do not need strict progression. Pick a useful weight, get clean reps, and move on. 
Special rules:

* Upper pull should appear regularly because it supports shoulder health and balances pressing. 
* Pulling can often handle slightly more volume than pushing. 
* In-season upper pull is usually safe to keep in, as long as it does not create excessive soreness or fatigue. 
* Rows and pulldowns are good year-round options. 
* Pull-ups are a good main upper pull option if the athlete is strong enough. 
* If shoulder, elbow, wrist, grip, rib, or lower-back issues are active, choose pulling variations that do not aggravate them. 
* If lower back is sore, use chest-supported rows or pulldowns instead of heavy bent-over rows. 
* If elbows are sore, reduce high-volume pull-ups/curls and use more controlled neutral-grip pulling. 
* Upper pull should not turn into a max-effort grip/back fatigue session right before a game.

Purpose
Power work helps the athlete express strength quickly.
It should support:
Acceleration
Jumping
Marking
Tackling
Breaking tackles
First-step quickness
Explosive upper-body contact
General athleticism
Power work is not conditioning. It should be fast, sharp and high quality.
Simple rule:
If power work becomes slow, sloppy or tiring, stop or reduce it.
Main power categories
Lower-body power:
Vertical jump
Box jump
Broad jump
Countermovement jump
Low box squat jump
RFE split squat jump
Medicine ball throw variations if available
Upper-body power:
Speed bench
Explosive push-ups
Explosive landmine press
Medicine ball chest pass if available
Medicine ball slam/throw if available
Contrast training
Contrast training means pairing a heavy strength movement with an explosive movement that uses a similar pattern.
The goal is to lift heavy, then immediately express power.
Example:
Heavy box squat
walk straight to
Vertical jump
Or:
Heavy bench press
walk straight to
Explosive push-up
There should be no long rest between the heavy lift and the explosive movement. The athlete should finish the heavy set, walk to the power movement, then do it sharply.
After the explosive movement, rest properly before the next contrast set.
Simple rule:
Heavy movement -> walk to explosive movement -> rest.
Good contrast pairings
Lower-body contrast examples:
Box squat -> vertical jump
Box squat -> box jump
Back squat -> broad jump
Trap bar deadlift -> broad jump
Split squat -> RFE split squat jump
Heavy sled push -> short acceleration
Upper-body contrast examples:
Bench press -> explosive push-up
Speed bench -> explosive push-up
Landmine press -> explosive landmine press
DB bench -> explosive push-up
Heavy row -> med ball slam/throw if available
Sets / reps for contrast training
Contrast training should stay low volume.
Simple guide:
2-4 contrast sets
Heavy lift: 2-4 reps
Power movement: 2-5 reps
Rest after the power movement
Example:
A1. Box squat - 3 x 3
A2. Vertical jump - 3 x 3
Rest after A2
Example:
A1. Bench press - 3 x 3-5
A2. Explosive push-up - 3 x 3-5
Rest after A2
When to use power work
Power work works best:
Early in the session
After warm-up
Before conditioning
When the athlete is fresh
When movement quality is high
It can be used as a primer before strength work, or as contrast training paired with a main lift.
Example primer:
Vertical jump 3 x 3
then
Box squat 3 x 3
Example contrast:
Box squat 3 x 3
paired with
Vertical jump 3 x 3
Intensity
Power work should feel explosive, not grindy.
RPE is not the main measure. Quality is the measure.
The athlete should feel:
Fast
Sharp
Bouncy
Coordinated
Fresh enough to repeat good reps
Do not chase fatigue, burn or pump.
Phase rules
In-season
Use only small doses.
In-season power work should be:
Low volume
Familiar
Explosive
Not fatiguing
Not soreness-building
Good in-season examples:
2-3 x 3 vertical jumps
2-3 x 3 box jumps
2-3 x 3 speed bench
2-3 x 3 explosive push-ups
Contrast training can be used in-season, but only in small, familiar doses.
Example:
Box squat 2-3 x 3
Vertical jump 2-3 x 3
This can be useful early in the week or as a tiny G-2 neural exposure for experienced athletes, but it should not become a full lower-body session.
Pre-season
Power work can be used more often, but still with controlled volume.
Good time to build:
Jump exposure
Acceleration power
Explosive pressing
Explosive lower-body intent
Contrast training
Be careful stacking hard jumps with sprinting, hard COD, heavy lower-body work or hard team training.
Off-season
Best time to build power properly because there is less game-day pressure.
Power work and contrast training can be paired with strength work and progressed gradually.
What to avoid
Do not:
Turn power into conditioning
Do high-rep jump circuits
Do jumps when the athlete is cooked
Do power work after hard conditioning
Do hard jumps close to game day
Do aggressive jumps with knee/calf/Achilles/ankle/groin issues
Chase soreness from power work
Use contrast training when the heavy lift becomes a grind
Use contrast training when the explosive movement gets slow
Bad examples:
5 rounds of 20 box jumps for time
High-rep broad jumps when tired
RFE split squat jumps close to game day
Jumps after heavy conditioning
Heavy squat to jump when squat reps are grinding
Bench to explosive push-up when the athlete is already fatigued

Knee issue
Calf/Achilles issue
Ankle issue
Groin issue
Hamstring issue
Hip issue
Lower-back issue
Avoid or reduce upper-body power if the athlete has active:
Shoulder issue
Elbow issue
Wrist issue
Rib/neck issue

Simple power rules
Power work should be fast, low-volume and high-quality.
Contrast training is:
heavy lift -> explosive movement -> rest.
Do not turn power into conditioning.
Do not chase fatigue.
Do not use power work when pain, soreness or poor readiness makes movement quality bad.
6. Conditioning rules
Conditioning philosophy
Conditioning should help the athlete play better footy, recover better, and handle the demands of games and team training.
The goal is not to randomly flog the athlete. The goal is to build or maintain the right engine for the time of year.
In-season conditioning should support performance and freshness. Pre-season conditioning should build game readiness. Off-season conditioning should build general fitness, aerobic base, and weak points without interfering too much with strength/muscle goals.
Team training and games count as conditioning load. The app should not ignore them.
Easy aerobic
Purpose: Build aerobic base, improve recovery, add low-stress conditioning, and help the athlete handle more total work without getting cooked.
Best modes: Easy run, bike, rower, ski, air bike, easy circuits.
Duration: Usually 15-45 minutes depending on phase, fitness level and weekly load.
Simple guide:

* Short flush: 15-25 min. 
* Normal easy aerobic: 25-40 min. 
* Longer aerobic base: 35-60 min if appropriate. 
* Can be continuous or easy intervals such as 3x8 min 2 min rest between rotating through different ergos. 
Intensity: Easy. Conversational. Around 3-6 / 10.
The athlete should finish feeling better or steady, not smashed.
When to use:

* Recovery day. 
* After hard games/training if the athlete needs movement. 
* Off-season aerobic base building. 
* Pre-season if field load is not already too high. 
* In-season if it does not interfere with game freshness. 
* When running load needs to be reduced but the athlete still needs conditioning. 
Progression:

* Add 5 minutes. 
* Add one extra easy aerobic day if recovery is good. Can also be optional. 
* Good way to add work in off-leg if they have hard sprint sessions or hard conditioning during the week on legs. With max 4 running sessions a 20 min bike flush or a 30 min easy circuit of bike ski row is a game changer. 
* Do not progress easy aerobic into a hard session by accident. 
Special rules:

* If the athlete is sore, cooked, injured or close to game day, easy aerobic should usually be off-feet. 
* Bike/ski/rower/air bike are good options when running load is already high. 
* If lower-limb injury is active, avoid running unless it is clearly tolerated. 
* If upper limb injury is active - avoid ski row and air bike unless clearly tolerated. 
Tempo / extensive conditioning
Purpose: Build repeat effort ability, running rhythm, aerobic power and work capacity without going as hard as true intervals.
This should sit between easy aerobic and hard conditioning.
Running examples:

* Tempo runs. 
* Cruise intervals. 
* Controlled shuttles. 
* Repeat 100s / 200s at controlled pace. 
* Footy-style run-throughs. 
* Extensive intervals where the athlete can keep quality. 
Bike/row/ski/air bike examples:

* 30 sec on / 30 sec easy. 
* 1 min on / 1 min easy. 
* 2 min on / 1 min easy. 
* Controlled 10-20 min blocks. 
* Moderate erg intervals. 
Work/rest:

* 1:1. 
* 2:1. 
* 3:1
* 3-5 min moderate blocks with easy recovery. 
Intensity: Usually 6-8 / 10.
Hard enough to train the engine, but not a max effort flogging.
When to use:

* Pre-season when building repeat effort capacity. 
* Off-season when trying to build fitness without smashing the athlete. 
* In-season only if there is enough room around game/team training. 
* As a middle-ground session when hard conditioning is too much but easy aerobic is not enough. 
Special rules:

* Avoid hard tempo running too close to game day. 
* Avoid stacking tempo running on top of hard team training in the same day. In fact avoid any conditioning the same day as footy training. 
* If lower-limb issues are active, use bike/rower/ski instead. 
* Tempo should not become a hidden sprint/COD session so monitoring intensity is important. 
Hard intervals
Purpose: Build high-output conditioning, VO2, repeat effort ability, glycolytic capacity and the ability to keep working under fatigue.
This is the “hard conditioning” bucket.
Examples:

* 4x4 min hard intervals. 
* 1km repeats. 
* MAS 15:15 style work. 
* 200m / 400m repeats. 
* Footy fartlek. 
* Assault bike intervals. 
* Rower/ski/bike hard intervals. 
* Erg EMOM. 
* Off-feet metcon. 
Work/rest examples:

* 4-5 x 4 min hard / 3 min easy. 
* 4-6 x 1km with controlled rest. up to 3 min rest. 
* 8 rounds blocks of 15 sec hard / 15 sec easy. 2 min rest between and repeat. 
* 8-12 x 30 sec hard / 30-60 sec easy. 
* 6-10 x 10-20 sec hard on assault bike/rower/ski. 
* 20-30 min total hard conditioning time depending on the athlete. 
How many reps: Depends on athlete level, phase, and what else is in the week.
Simple guide:

* Poor fitness: fewer reps, more rest. 
* Average: standard prescription. 
* Good/very fit: more reps, slightly less rest, or higher quality output. 
* In-season: keep dose conservative. 
* Pre-season/off-season: more room to build. 
* Most hard sessions like this should be 20-30min or so. Maybe a little more sometimes.
Intensity: Usually 8-9 / 10.
Hard, but not random death. The athlete should still be able to complete the session with decent quality.
When not to use:

* Within 48 hours of game day. 
* Day after game. 
* Too close to hard team training. 
* When the athlete is sick, cooked, or injured. 
* When lower-body strength is already heavy and fatigue is high. 
* When the athlete has active hamstring/groin/calf/knee/ankle issues and the session is running-based. 
Progression:

* Add one rep. 
* Add one interval. 
* Add 5 minutes total work. 
* Slightly reduce rest. Careful with this one. I'd rather keep the rest period solid and have them work harder. 
* Increase output/pace only if the athlete is coping. 
* Do not progress multiple variables at once. 
* In-season should mostly maintain, not aggressively build. 
Special rules:

* Hard conditioning is a hard exposure. 
* It should count alongside games, team training, lower strength and sprint work. 
* If team training is already hard, the app should be careful adding hard intervals. 
* If the athlete says hard conditioning is brutal repeatedly, reduce volume or swap to easier/off-feet work. 
Off-feet conditioning
When to use:

* When running load is already high. 
* In-season when the athlete needs conditioning without extra leg pounding. 
* When lower-limb soreness or injury is active. 
* When the athlete has team training/game load already. 
* When conditioning is needed but sprint/COD/running is not appropriate. 
Bike: Good all-round option. Useful for hard intervals, aerobic flushes, recovery rides, and leg-sparing conditioning.
Rower: Good engine option, but can load the back/hips/hamstrings, so be careful with lower-back or hamstring issues. Don't program more than 10 minute rounds on this. Prefer max of 4-8min at any one time to be sitting on a rower. 
Ski: Good option when lower-limb load needs to be reduced. Can be useful for upper-body conditioning and aerobic flushes. Don't program more than 10 minute rounds on this. Prefer max of 4-8min at any one time using ski erg. 
Assault bike: Good for hard intervals, short power work, and off-feet conditioning. Can get brutal quickly, so dose matters. Best one for sprints as it uses whole body and requires 0 skill. 
Why it matters: Off-feet conditioning lets the app keep the athlete fit without constantly adding more running load.
This is especially useful for local footy because games and team training already include running, sprinting, deceleration, change of direction and contact.
Conditioning by phase
In-season
Goal: Maintain fitness, support recovery, and protect game-day freshness.
Rules:

* Game day and team training are the main conditioning exposures. 
* Extra hard conditioning should be limited. Can be used if missing a training session due to factors unrelated to injury, illness, or fatigue. 
* Most extra conditioning should be easy, recovery-based, or off-feet. 
* No hard conditioning within 48 hours of game day. 
* Be careful adding hard running if the athlete already has team training. 
* Bye weeks can allow a little more work if the athlete is fresh. 
* If the athlete is cooked, sick, sore or injured, reduce conditioning first before smashing strength. 
Good in-season conditioning:

* Easy bike flush. 
* Short zone 2 off-feet. 
* Light tempo if there is enough space from game day. 
* Very small hard conditioning dose only when appropriate. Such as air bike sprints 3x20 seconds flat out every 2 min or 6x10 second every minute. 
* Easy circuits such as EMOM's, 30:30x30, 8 on 2 off x 3 rounds, etc. 
Pre-season
Goal: Build game readiness, repeat effort capacity, and running/conditioning tolerance while respecting team training load.
Rules:

* Team training is a major load anchor. 
* Do not add hard conditioning on team training days. 
* Do not stack hard running too close to team training if the athlete is already doing lots of field work. i.e. team training monday and wednesday then friday could be hard conditioning and saturday a long slow run or something. 
* More hard conditioning is allowed than in-season, but load still needs to be managed. 
* Use a mix of aerobic base, tempo, hard intervals, and sprint exposure. Ideal week looks like speed exposure, repeat sprint ability, hard conditioning, and low slow aerobic base building. Some of that you get during training and some you won't. 
* Build progressively. Do not jump from nothing to a huge running load. 
Good pre-season conditioning:

* Tempo intervals. 
* VO2 intervals. 
* Repeat-effort work. 
* Easy aerobic base. 
* Off-feet conditioning when legs are overloaded. 
* MAS running. 
* 1km repeats. 
Off-season
Goal: Build aerobic base, improve general fitness, support body composition goals, and prepare the athlete for harder pre-season work.
Rules:

* More room to build because there is no game day to protect. 
* Conditioning should not destroy strength/hypertrophy goals unless the athlete prioritises fitness/fat loss. 
* Aerobic base can be built more consistently. 
* Hard intervals can be included, but not every session needs to be hard. 
* Running can be built gradually if the athlete tolerates it. 
* Good time to fix weak engine without the pressure of games. 
Good off-season conditioning:

* Zone 2 work. 
* Tempo intervals. 
* Bike/row/ski/air bike intervals. 
* Occasional hard conditioning. 
* Gradual running exposure. 
* A good mix of stimulus is useful here, i.e. not just all aerobic base, still good to get some intensity but potentially having longer interval sessions vs pure speed work. 
* Speed work can come closer to the start of pre season so you're ready for speed/COD at training.
Conditioning by athlete level
Poor conditioning

* Start easier than the athlete thinks they need. 
* More easy aerobic and controlled tempo. 
* Less hard interval volume. 
* Longer rests. 
* Shorter total sessions. 
* More off-feet options if running breaks them down. 
* Progress slowly. 
Example:

* 15-25 min easy aerobic. 
* 6-8 controlled intervals instead of 12-15. 
* More bike/row/ski/assault bike. 
Average conditioning

* Standard LFA conditioning dose. 
* Mix easy aerobic, tempo and some hard intervals. 
* Can handle moderate progression if recovery is good. 
* Still avoid stacking too much with team training/game day. 
Good conditioning

* Can handle more volume or better-quality intervals. 
* Can progress with more reps, slightly shorter rest, or more running-specific work. 
* Still needs protection around game day. 
* Should not be punished with endless extra conditioning just because they are fit. 
Very fit

* Needs enough stimulus to maintain or improve. 
* May need harder or more specific conditioning to feel challenged. 
* Still should not overdo hard work in-season. 
* Can use conditioning to sharpen, but not at the cost of freshness. 
* If their main goal is strength/size, do not over-prescribe conditioning just because they can tolerate it. 
* Focus on quality over quantity. 
Conditioning feedback rules
If athlete says too easy

* First check the phase. 
* In-season: maybe leave it if freshness is the priority. 
* Pre/off-season: slightly increase volume, reps, time, or intensity. 
* Do not make a huge jump from one session. 
* If repeated over multiple sessions, progress the next block. 
Possible changes:

* Add 1 rep. 
* Add 5 minutes. 
* Slightly reduce rest. 
* Move from easy aerobic to tempo. 
* Move from low-end to normal dose. 
If athlete says too hard

* Reduce volume first. 
* Increase rest. 
* Swap running to off-feet. 
* Drop from hard intervals to tempo or easy aerobic. 
* Check if team training/game load is already high. 
* If repeated, reduce future conditioning dose. 
Possible changes:

* Remove 1-2 reps. 
* Reduce total duration. 
* Use bike/ski/row instead of running. 
* Replace hard conditioning with easy aerobic or recovery. 
If athlete fails session

* Do not punish them. 
* Treat it as useful feedback. 
* Hold or reduce the next conditioning dose. 
* If they failed because of time, offer a shorter version. 
* If they failed because it was too hard, regress. 
* If they failed because of soreness/injury, adjust around the issue. 
If athlete is sore after

* If soreness is mild, keep going but monitor. 
* If lower-limb soreness is high, reduce running, sprinting, jumping and hard intervals. 
* Use off-feet conditioning. 
* Avoid hard conditioning close to game day. 
* If soreness repeats, reduce dose or change modality. 
If conditioning repeatedly feels brutal

* Lower the base dose. 
* Use more easy aerobic. 
* Use fewer hard intervals. 
* Increase rest. 
* Use more off-feet conditioning. 
* Check recent training load, game load, sleep, illness and injury. 
* Create a Coach Note if the program is being actively reduced. 
Simple rule:
One bad session = adjust slightly.
Repeated bad sessions = change the programming.
Simple conditioning rules

* Conditioning should support footy, not ruin the athlete. 
* Team training and games count as conditioning. 
* In-season conditioning should protect freshness. 
* Pre-season conditioning can push harder but must respect team training. 
* Off-season conditioning can build more steadily. 
* Hard conditioning is a hard day. 
* Easy aerobic should feel easy. 
* Off-feet conditioning is useful when running load is high. 
* Lower-limb injuries should shift conditioning away from running. 
* If the athlete says conditioning is too hard repeatedly, the app must respond. 
* Footy athletes must be able to sprint, repeat spring, grind for up to 1 min, have a big engine as well as play for 2 hours. So conditioning should prepare them for all of that. 
Conditioning finisher rules
Conditioning finishers are small add-ons, not hidden second sessions.
A finisher should have a clear purpose:

* easy recovery / flush
* aerobic base touch
* true tempo / moderate conditioning
* trunk / carry / robustness work
* low-impact conditioning when running load is already high
Not every strength session needs a finisher.
The app should not automatically add conditioning to every strength day just to fill space. If the week already has enough conditioning exposure, or the finisher does not improve the week, it can be skipped.
Avoid boring default prescriptions.
LFA should not repeatedly prescribe the same 15-20 minute straight row, ski or bike finishers across the week.
Long easy aerobic work can be useful, but it should not be the default finisher every time. It should be used when it has a clear purpose.
Preferred finisher variety:

* 8-12 min easy flush
* 10-15 min aerobic intervals
* 30:30 controlled tempo blocks
* 1 min on / 1 min easy tempo
* 2 min on / 1 min easy bike/row/ski
* short loaded carries
* trunk circuits
* mobility / prehab circuits
* no finisher when the week does not need one
Lower body days:

* usually easy off-feet aerobic, short flush, mobility, trunk or no finisher
* avoid hard conditioning after heavy lower
* avoid sprint/COD after hinge-heavy work
Upper body days:

* good place for tempo or moderate conditioning
* can also use trunk, carries or aerobic intervals
* still avoid hidden VO2/sprint unless specifically planned
Game/team training weeks:

* team training and games already count as conditioning/sprint exposure
* do not add hard finishers near team training or games
* G-1 should stay light
Simple rule: A finisher should make the week better, not just make the session longer.
Important distinction:
A finisher is not the same as a conditioning component.
A finisher is a small add-on.
A conditioning component is proper planned conditioning work that may sit on the same day as strength.
For low-availability athletes, especially 4-day off-season athletes, conditioning often needs to be paired with strength days.
The app should not remove useful conditioning just because it is attached to a strength session.
The problem is not “conditioning after strength”.
The problem is low-value filler finishers repeated across the week.
Simple rule:
Use as much useful conditioning as the athlete can recover from, but do not pad sessions with repetitive junk.
7. Sprint, speed and COD rules
Sprint / speed philosophy
Sprint work should help the athlete stay exposed to high-speed running, acceleration, deceleration and footy-specific movement without creating unnecessary injury risk or fatigue.
Sprint work is not the same as hard conditioning.
True speed work should be high quality, low fatigue and done with enough rest. If the athlete is gassed, slowing down badly, or just trying to survive the session, it has probably turned into conditioning.
In-season, most sprint exposure should usually come from team training and games. Extra sprint work should only be added if the athlete needs it, has room in the week, and is not close to game day. Might be worth adding a note to team training sessions to make sure the athlete tries to get in at least 3-4 30-40m close to max speed sprints in at some stage if the rest of the group is not doing it. 
Pre-season and off-season allow more room to build sprint exposure properly.
Acceleration
Purpose: Improve the athlete’s ability to get moving quickly from a stop or low speed. Useful for leading, chasing, closing space, tackling, reacting and short bursts in footy.
Distances: Usually 5-30m.
Simple examples:

* 6-10 x 10m. 
* 4-8 x 20m. 
* 4-6 x 30m. 
* Hill sprints or sled pushes can be used carefully. 
Reps: Usually 4-10 quality reps depending on phase, athlete level and weekly load.
Rest: Enough to keep quality high.

* Short acceleration: 20-30m
* Longer acceleration: 30-50m 
* If speed drops off badly, stop or reduce reps. 
When to use:

* Off-season to build speed qualities. 
* Pre-season to prepare for footy demands. 
* Early in the week if in-season and needed. Don't add sprint work if they are training 2 times or more at the club. We will make note on team training days to make sure they are getting in speed work. If they only train once then they should be completing a warm up with sprints at the end of it before further conditioning to make up for missing training = if they are healthy and fresh. 
* When the athlete does not get enough sprint exposure at team training. 
* When the athlete is fresh enough to move well. 
* This can be used as a warm up to other running sessions. 
When to avoid:

* Close to game day. 
* Day after game. 
* When hamstring, calf, Achilles, groin, knee or ankle issues are active. 
* When the athlete is sick, cooked, sore or not moving well. 
* After heavy lower-body work if it will reduce quality. 
Special rules:

* Acceleration should be crisp, not grindy. 
* Do it before fatigue work, not after. 
* Quality matters more than volume. 
* Should not be breathing too heavy. Should be focused on quality and bouncing off the ground. 
Max velocity
Purpose: Expose the athlete to high-speed running so they maintain or improve top-end speed and hamstring tolerance.
This is important because footy exposes players to high-speed running whether they train it or not.
Distances: Usually flying sprint style.
Examples:

* 20m build + 20m fast. 
* 30m build + 30m fast. 
* 40-60m smooth sprint exposure. Gradually building to top end speed. 
* 3-5 flying sprints. 
Reps: Usually 3-6 high-quality reps.
Rest: Long enough to stay fast.

* 2-4 min between reps. 
* More rest if needed. 
* Stop if speed drops off or mechanics look poor. 
When to use:

* Off-season and pre-season as a proper speed exposure. 
* In-season only as a micro-dose if needed and not already covered by team/game. Same rules as accelerations. May not need in season because get enough at training. 
* Earlier in the week, away from game day. 
* When the athlete is fresh. 
* This can be used as a warm up to other running sessions. 
When to avoid:

* Hamstring issue. 
* Groin issue. 
* Calf/Achilles issue. 
* Athlete is cooked, sick, sore or carrying lower-limb pain. 
* Too close to game day. 
* If team training already had plenty of high-speed running. 
Special rules:

* Max velocity is not conditioning. 
* Full recovery between reps. 
* Low volume, high quality. 
* Better to do too little than too much. 
Change of direction
Purpose: COD is important for footy, but LFA does not usually prescribe dedicated COD/agility sessions. Most local footballers already get enough cutting, reacting, braking and changing direction through team training and games.
The app should not waste training time trying to turn COD into a major standalone quality unless there is a clear reason.
Main philosophy: Strength training, sprint exposure, good lower-body strength, trunk control, single-leg work, and regular footy training should cover most COD needs.
I do not believe most local athletes will make big enough improvements in agility or COD from isolated cone drills to make it worth prioritising over strength, conditioning, sprinting, recovery and staying available.
When COD might appear: Only occasionally, usually:

* Tail end of off-season. 
* Christmas / January gap before full team training returns. 
* When the athlete is healthy, fresh and not close to games. 
* As a small conditioning-style finisher, not a main training priority. 
Example session:
Up-back shuttle
30m sprint out
180-degree turn
30m sprint back
Walk up and back to recover
Repeat for 10-15 minutes after a warm-up
When to avoid:

* In-season, unless there is a very specific reason. 
* Close to game day. 
* When team training already includes plenty of COD. 
* When groin, knee, ankle, calf/Achilles, hip or hamstring issues are active. 
* When the athlete is sore, cooked, sick or not moving well. 
* After heavy lower-body fatigue. 
Special rules:

* COD should not be a major programmed category for LFA. 
* Do not add random cone drills just to look “sport-specific.” 
* Do not use COD as filler when strength, conditioning or recovery would be more useful. 
* If COD is used, keep it simple, controlled and low volume. 
* Hard COD counts as a hard lower-body exposure. 
* Footy training and games are the main COD exposure.
Repeated sprint
Purpose: Build the ability to repeat high-speed efforts with incomplete recovery, which is useful for footy repeat efforts.
This is closer to conditioning than pure speed.
Examples:

* 10-15 x 20m with walk back as rest 
* 10 x 30m with walk-back recovery. 
* 3-5 sets of 3-5 short sprints. 
* Sprint / jog / walk repeat patterns. 
* Assault bike 3x20 second max sprints start every 2 min, or 6x10 second max sprints starting every minute
* Rower or ski erg repeat sprint intervals if running load needs to be reduced - 20-30 second sprints every 90 seconds or 2 minutes repeat for 10-15 min. 
Reps: Depends on phase and athlete level.
Simple guide:

* Low level: fewer reps, more rest. 
* Average: moderate reps. 
* Very fit: more reps or slightly shorter rest. 
* In-season: small dose only if needed. 
Rest: Shorter than true speed work, but still enough to keep reasonable quality.
Examples:

* 20-60 sec between reps and up to 2 minutes if absolutely flat out
* 2-4 min between sets. 
When to use:

* Pre-season. 
* Off-season if conditioning is a goal. 
* In-season only if the week has room and the athlete is fresh. 
* Bye week if extra work is appropriate. 
How close to game day: Avoid close to game day.
As a rough rule:

* Avoid within 48 hours of a game. 
* Be careful within 72 hours if the session is hard. 
* Earlier in the week is better in-season. 
* Would do off leg only on a monday or wednesday in season unless missing team trainings
Special rules:

* Repeated sprint is a hard exposure. 
* It should count alongside hard conditioning, team training, lower-body strength and game day. 
* If sprint quality drops badly, stop or reduce volume. 
* If lower-limb issue is active, shift to off-feet intervals or remove. 
Sprint exposure by phase
In-season
Goal: Maintain sprint exposure and stay prepared for game speed without adding unnecessary fatigue or injury risk.
Rules:

* Team training and games usually provide most sprint exposure. 
* Extra sprint work should be limited. 
* Do not add hard sprint/COD close to game day. 
* If sprint exposure is missing, use a small micro-dose early in the week. 
* Avoid sprint work if hamstring/groin/calf/knee/ankle issues are active. 
* Do not chase big sprint gains in-season if it risks freshness. 
Good in-season examples:

* 3-4 x 10-20m accelerations. can be done in warm up at team training
* 3 x flying 10-20m. can be done in warm up at team training
* Low-volume, high-quality exposure. 
* Only if the athlete is fresh and the week allows it. 
* Prioritise ergo sprints over sprint running in season
Pre-season
Goal: Build sprint, acceleration, deceleration and COD tolerance for footy.
Rules:

* Sprint exposure matters, but team training already creates load. 
* Avoid placing sprint work the day before or after hard team training. 
* Build volume gradually. 
* Include acceleration, max velocity and COD across the phase. 
* Do not stack sprint/COD with hard lower strength too aggressively. 
* If team training is hard and running-heavy, reduce extra sprint work. 
Good pre-season examples:

* Acceleration session. 
* Max velocity exposure. 
* Repeated sprint session. 
* Small doses combined with strength where appropriate. 
Off-season
Goal: Build or rebuild speed qualities with less pressure from games.
Rules:

* Best time to build sprint mechanics, acceleration and max velocity. tail end of off season just before entering pre season - should not start absolutley flat out if they haven't trained speed since footy finished months ago. 
* More room to train speed because there is no game day to protect. 
* Still progress gradually. 
* Do not turn every sprint session into conditioning. 
* Good time to build tolerance before pre-season. 
Good off-season examples:

* Acceleration work. 
* Flying sprints. 
* Hill sprints. Starting with hill sprints can be best. Can't run as fast and good for mechanics. 
* Deceleration and landing work. 
* Low-to-moderate COD progressions. 
* Repeated sprint only if conditioning is a goal. 
Sprint exposure by athlete
Already sprints at training

* Count team training as sprint exposure. 
* Do not automatically add more sprint work. 
* Use micro-doses only if needed. 
* Focus on strength, recovery and filling gaps. 
* If they are sore from team training, reduce extra sprint/COD. 
Does no sprint work

* Add exposure gradually. 
* Start with acceleration and low-volume max velocity. 
* Avoid jumping straight to hard repeated sprint or aggressive COD. 
* Begin with small doses: 
   * 3-6 accelerations. 
   * 2-4 flying sprints. 
   * Low-intensity deceleration drills. 
* Progress slowly. Can start as part of their warm-up before other running sessions. 
Hamstring / groin history

* Be conservative with sprint volume. 
* Avoid sudden max velocity spikes. 
* Avoid aggressive COD if groin is a concern. 
* Use proper spacing from games and team training. 
* Keep exposures small and consistent rather than random big sessions. 
* If symptoms are active, reduce or remove sprint/COD. 
Poor conditioning

* Do not use sprint work as conditioning straight away. 
* Keep speed work low volume and high quality. 
* Build aerobic/tempo base separately. 
* Repeated sprint work should come later. 
* Use more rest. 
* Stop before mechanics fall apart. 
Very fit

* Can handle more repeat-effort work, but still needs protection from overload. 
* Do not add sprint/COD just because they are fit. 
* Keep true speed work high quality. 
* Repeated sprint can be harder if the phase supports it. 
* In-season still prioritises freshness. 
Simple sprint/COD rules

* Sprint work is high quality, not random fatigue. 
* COD is a hard lower-body exposure when done hard. 
* In-season sprint exposure usually comes from team training and games. 
* Pre-season builds sprint/COD tolerance. 
* Off-season is best for developing speed qualities. 
* Avoid sprint/COD close to game day. 
* Avoid sprint/COD with active hamstring, groin, calf, Achilles, knee or ankle issues. 
* If the athlete has not sprinted recently, start small. 
* If sprint quality drops, stop the session. 
* Do not turn every speed session into conditioning
* Can be used at the end of warm-ups before other sessions if appropriate. 
* Hill sprints can be a good introduction. 
8. Injury rules
Injury philosophy
LFA is not a rehab app, physio app or medical advice app.
The app should not diagnose injuries or prescribe rehab. The goal is to adjust strength and conditioning around what the athlete can tolerate.
The app should:

* avoid obvious aggravators 
* reduce load, volume, speed, range or impact when needed 
* keep safe training in the program where possible 
* stop affected training when symptoms are high 
* recommend medical or physio advice when the issue is moderate/severe, unclear or getting worse 
The goal is not “injury = stop everything.”
The goal is:
Do as much useful work as possible without making the issue worse.
Exercise swap hierarchy
When an exercise hurts or is not tolerated, the app should use this order:
Same movement pattern if possible
 Example: barbell bench press -> DB floor press. 
Same or similar muscle group if the movement pattern is not tolerated
 Example: pull-ups hurt -> chest-supported row or pulldown. 
Unaffected body area if the area needs a break
 Example: shoulder issue -> lower body, bike, core, easy conditioning. But be careful not adding too much load to the other areas that are already working during the rest of the week. 
Recovery / easy conditioning if strength work is not appropriate 
Pause affected training and recommend advice if issue is severe 
Bad swaps are swaps that are basically the same aggravating stress.
Example:
Bad swap:
Bench press hurts -> lighter bench press
Better swap:
Bench press hurts -> neutral-grip DB floor press, push-up, landmine press, or lower-body/core if pressing is not tolerated
General severity rules
1-3 / 10
Mild issue.

* Keep most training the same. 
* Avoid only the exact movement or trigger that flares it up. 
* Keep strength, conditioning and sprint exposure if tolerated. 
* Use small swaps if needed. 
* No major rebuild unless symptoms keep repeating. 
4-5 / 10
Moderate annoyance.

* Reduce load, volume, range, speed or intensity slightly. 
* Swap obvious aggravators. 
* Keep safe work in. 
* Avoid hard exposure directly through the affected area. 
* Good time to suggest physio/medical advice if it is not improving. 
6-7 / 10
Limiting issue.

* Tell the athlete to get physio/medical advice. 
* Moderately reduce affected work. 
* Avoid movements that directly trigger it. 
* Reduce or remove high-speed, heavy, high-impact or high-volume work through that area. 
* Keep unaffected work in where possible. 
* Use off-feet conditioning if lower limb is affected. 
* Create a clear Coach Note so the athlete knows the program is being adjusted. 
8-10 / 10
Bad issue.

* Pause affected training. 
* Do not try to work around it aggressively. 
* Recommend medical or physio advice. 
* Use rest, recovery, or clearly unaffected training only. 
* If the issue is back, neck, head, chest, severe lower limb, or the athlete cannot move normally, the app may need to stop training entirely for a bit. 
Serious issue rule
LFA does not diagnose. LFA does not rehab. LFA adjusts S&C.
The app should stop affected training and recommend physio/medical advice when:

* pain is 8-10 / 10 
* pain is sharp, worsening or unclear 
* athlete cannot walk, lift, run or move normally 
* numbness or tingling is present 
* head, neck, chest, dizziness or breathing symptoms are present 
* the athlete reports a pop, tear, giving way or major loss of function 
* symptoms are not improving over time 
* any movement makes the issue worse 
For these cases, the app should not send them to AI chat as the solution. It should pause affected work and tell them to get proper advice.

Reintroduction rules
When the athlete wants to reintroduce affected work, the app should not use AI chat by default.
It should use the tap system:
Update injury
What can you do now?
What still triggers it?
How much is it limiting you?
If severity improves:

* 8-10 -> 6-7: move from paused affected work to cautious alternatives. 
* 6-7 -> 4-5: reintroduce low volume and controlled movement. 
* 4-5 -> 1-3: reintroduce more normal work but avoid big jumps. 
* 1-3 -> clear: return to normal program gradually. 
Simple rule:
Reintroduce the easiest, lowest-risk version first.
Then add load, volume, speed or range one at a time.
Do not reintroduce everything at once.
Examples:

* Hamstring: bike -> easy run -> acceleration -> max velocity. 
* Groin: squeeze/isometric -> controlled lower -> straight-line run -> COD/kicking. 
* Shoulder: neutral DB/floor press -> push-up/landmine -> bench/OHP. 
* Lower back: supported upper/bike -> controlled core -> lower strength -> hinge/loading. 
* Calf/Achilles: bike -> walk/jog -> calf strength -> running -> sprint/jump. 
Simple injury rules

* Avoid the obvious thing that aggravates it. 
* Reduce load/volume when symptoms are moderate. 
* Pause affected work when symptoms are high. 
* Keep safe work in the program where possible. 
* Aim for same movement pattern first, then same muscle group, then unaffected training. 
* Do not swap to the same aggravating exercise with a slightly different name. 
* Anything 6/10 or above should recommend physio/medical advice. 
* 8-10 / 10 means pause affected training. 
* Severe back, neck, head, chest, dizziness, numbness/tingling, or cannot-walk issues may mean stop training entirely until checked. 
* Reintroduce through the tap system, not open-ended AI chat.
9. Fatigue, sickness and readiness rules
Tired today
Rule: If the athlete is tired today, reduce the session slightly rather than deleting everything.
What to change:

* Reduce volume. 
* Remove finishers. 
* Swap hard conditioning for easy recovery. 
* Keep main lifts if they can still move well. 
* Lower intensity if needed. 
Example: Upper Push becomes a shorter upper session with fewer accessories.
Cooked this week
Rule: If the athlete is cooked for the week, reduce the whole week’s load.
What to change:

* Reduce hard sessions. 
* Remove extra conditioning. 
* Swap one or more sessions to recovery/rest. 
* Keep the most important session if possible. 
* Protect game day freshness. 
Coach Note: “Load reduced this week”
Sick
Rule: If the athlete is sick, reduce training load and avoid hard sessions.
What to change:

* No hard conditioning. 
* No max sprinting. 
* No heavy lower if they feel flat. 
* Use rest, recovery, easy bike, mobility or very light strength only if appropriate. 
Simple rule: If they feel mildly off, reduce. If they feel properly sick, rest.
Coach Note: “Recovery mode active”
Bedridden
Rule: If the athlete is bedridden, stop training for now.
What to change:

* Clear hard sessions. 
* Replace with rest/recovery. 
* Do not try to “train through it.” 
* Program can rebuild once they are good again. 
Coach Note: “Recovery mode active”
Sore
Rule: If the athlete is sore, adjust based on where and how bad.
What to change:

* Mild soreness: keep training but avoid extra volume. 
* Moderate soreness: reduce affected work. 
* Severe soreness: swap to recovery/rest or unaffected training. 
* If lower body is sore, reduce sprinting, hard conditioning and heavy lower. 
* If upper body is sore, keep lower/body conditioning if appropriate. 
Coach Note: Only create one if soreness changes the program.
Poor sleep
Rule: Poor sleep should reduce intensity before it deletes the whole session.
What to change:

* Keep session if it is important and athlete feels okay. 
* Reduce volume or accessories. 
* Avoid max effort work. 
* Avoid hard conditioning if sleep has been bad for multiple nights. 
* If poor sleep combines with soreness/sickness, reduce more aggressively. 
Simple rule: One bad sleep = small adjustment. Repeated bad sleep = reduce load.
Busy week
Rule: If the athlete has a busy week, reduce the program to the most important work.
What to change:

* Keep the key strength session if possible. 
* Keep game/team training anchors. 
* Remove optional extras. 
* Remove extra conditioning first. 
* Use shorter sessions where possible. 
* Create more rest days if needed. 
Coach Note: “Busy week adjustment active”
Away / holiday
Rule: If the athlete is away, clear or reduce the affected days.
What to change:

* If they cannot train, make those days rest. 
* If they can do light work, use bodyweight, walking, easy cardio, mobility or recovery. 
* Do not force normal gym programming if they are away from equipment. 
* Restore normal programming when the away period is cleared. 
Coach Note: “Away adjustment active”
Missed session
Rule: If the athlete misses one session, do not panic or punish them.
Options:

* Mark it as missed. 
* Move it forward if it still makes sense. 
* Skip it if the week is already full. 
* Replace it with recovery if they are cooked. 
Simple rule: Missing one session should not wreck the week.
Missed multiple sessions
Rule: If the athlete misses multiple sessions, the app should reassess the week.
What to change:

* Do not cram every missed session back in. 
* Pick the most important work. 
* Reduce the week if needed. 
* Restart momentum with a realistic session. 
* Avoid sudden catch-up overload. 
Simple rule: Do not “make up” missed work by smashing the athlete.
What creates Coach Note
A Coach Note should be created when something is actively changing the program.
Examples:

* Sick / recovery mode. 
* Cooked / load reduced. 
* Busy week. 
* Away days. 
* Injury restriction. 
* Equipment limitation. 
* Future exercise preference. 
* Missed session adjustment if it changes the week. 
Do not create Coach Notes for normal profile info like name, position, season phase, normal game day or normal training days.
What clears Coach Note
A Coach Note should clear when the athlete says the issue is no longer active.
Examples:

* “I’m good now” 
* “I’m all good now” 
* “I’ve been cleared” 
* “Clear adjustment” 
* “Back to normal” 
* Away/busy period has ended 
* Equipment limitation removed 
Clearing the note should stop that issue affecting the program.
What should auto-expire
Some short-term things should disappear naturally.
Could auto-expire:

* Tired today: end of day. 
* Poor sleep: end of day unless repeated. 
* Mild soreness: end of day or after next check-in. 
* Missed session prompt: once answered. 
* Busy week: end of selected week. 
* Away/holiday: end of selected date range. 
Should not auto-expire too quickly:

* Injury. 
* Equipment limitation. 
* Future exercise preference. 
* Serious training pause. 
* Ongoing sickness if user has not said they are better. 
What requires rebuild
A rebuild is needed when the structure of the week changes.
Requires rebuild/update:

* Sick/bedridden clears or reduces multiple days. 
* Busy week reduces the week. 
* Away/holiday clears selected days. 
* Injury changes what sessions/exercises are allowed. 
* User clears an injury/recovery/busy/away Coach Note. 
* Multiple missed sessions change the week. 
* Game day/team training/training days change. 
Does not always need full rebuild:

* One session is marked complete. 
* One session is skipped. 
* One exercise is swapped today only. 
* One accessory is removed today only. 
* Mild tiredness reduces today only. 
Load reduction rules
Slight reduction
Use when:

* Tired today. 
* Mild soreness. 
* One bad sleep. 
* 1-3 / 10 issue. 
* Session feels a bit much. 
What to do:

* Remove 1-2 accessories. 
* Reduce sets slightly. 
* Keep main lift but do not push. 
* Swap hard finisher for easy work. 
* Keep the day mostly intact. 
Moderate reduction
Use when:

* Cooked. 
* Poor sleep for multiple nights. 
* 4-5 / 10 injury issue. 
* Hard week at work. 
* Training feels too much. 
What to do:

* Remove hard conditioning. 
* Reduce volume across the session. 
* Swap lower stress alternatives. 
* Turn one hard session into recovery. 
* Keep only the most important work. 
Major reduction
Use when:

* Sick. 
* Very cooked. 
* 6-7 / 10 injury issue. 
* Multiple missed sessions. 
* Busy week with low availability. 
What to do:

* Remove most hard work. 
* Keep recovery, easy aerobic or unaffected training. 
* Avoid sprinting, hard conditioning and heavy lower. 
* Rebuild the week around getting through safely. 
Full pause
Use when:

* Bedridden. 
* 8-10 / 10 injury issue. 
* Severe sickness. 
* Serious injury symptoms. 
* Athlete cannot train normally. 
What to do:

* Pause affected training. 
* Use rest/recovery. 
* Recommend physio/medical advice where appropriate. 
* Rebuild when the athlete says they are ready or cleared.

New to training

4-8 reps
controlled technique
not close to failure

* Poor conditioning builds gradually. 
* Very fit athletes need enough stimulus, but still need game-day freshness. 
* Low recent training starts conservative. 
* High recent training needs load management.
12. Goals rules
Main idea
Goals help the app understand what the athlete cares about, but they should not completely change the program.
Most local footy athletes need the same basics: strength, conditioning, sprint exposure, recovery, and injury management.
Goals should slightly bias the program, the language, and the emphasis, but they should never override:
season phase
game day freshness
team training load
injuries
fatigue
available days
Simple rule:
Goals steer the program slightly. They do not rewrite the whole program.
Make the senior team
Bias: Train consistently, build physical standards, stay available, and improve the main qualities that help selection.
More of:

* Consistency 
* Strength basics 
* Conditioning 
* Recovery habits 
* Weak point work 
Less of:

* Random optional extras 
* Training that leaves them too sore to train/play 
* Big risky jumps in load 
Session bias: Balanced program. Strength + conditioning + recovery.
What the app should not do: Do not make the program extreme. The athlete needs to train well, recover well, and be available.
Dominate your level
Bias: Push performance slightly more when the athlete is coping well.
More of:

* Strength and power 
* Harder conditioning when appropriate 
* Sprint exposure when appropriate 
* High-quality main lifts 
Less of:

* Overly cautious programming when the athlete is healthy and recovering well 
* Junk volume 
Session bias: Slightly more performance-focused. Still respect game day and injuries.
What the app should not do: Do not turn every week into a flogging. Dominating still requires freshness.
Feel fresh on game day
Bias: Protect game-day freshness above chasing extra work.
More of:

* Smart session placement 
* Recovery 
* Low-fatigue strength maintenance 
* Easy aerobic / flush work 
* Load management 
Less of:

* Hard conditioning near game day 
* High-volume lower-body work in-season 
* Extra sessions just for the sake of it 
Session bias: Maintenance, freshness, recovery, smart strength.
What the app should not do: Do not add hard work late in the week unless the athlete clearly chooses to override.
Stay injury-free
Bias: Prioritise availability, tissue capacity, and avoiding spikes in load.
More of:

* Consistent strength basics 
* Hamstring, adductor, calf, trunk work, shoulder prehab 
* Recovery 
* Gradual progressions 
* Load warnings 
Less of:

* Sudden volume spikes 
* Hard sprint/COD jumps 
* Random new exercises in-season 
* Excessive fatigue 
Session bias: Strength + prehab/accessories + sensible conditioning.
What the app should not do: Do not make the program soft, but avoid reckless jumps.
Get stronger and fitter
Bias: Balanced physical development.
More of:

* Main lifts 
* Conditioning progression 
* Full-body robustness 
* Consistent weekly structure 
Less of:

* Too much focus on only one quality 
* Random pump work that doesn’t support the goal 
Session bias: Strength and conditioning both matter.
What the app should not do: Do not over-bias strength so fitness drops, or over-bias conditioning so strength stalls.
Build muscle
Bias: Slightly more hypertrophy volume where the phase allows.
More of:

* 6-10 rep work 
* Accessories 
* Upper/lower volume 
* Arms, delts, back, legs 
* Consistent lifting 
Less of:

* Excess hard conditioning if it interferes with size 
* Too much low-rep-only strength work 
* Excess running volume in off-season if size is the priority 
Session bias: More strength/hypertrophy, especially off-season.
What the app should not do: Do not chase muscle in-season at the cost of soreness and game-day freshness.
Stay consistent
Bias: Make the program realistic and easy to keep doing.
More of:

* Simple sessions 
* Repeatable structure 
* Manageable volume 
* Clear priorities 
* Adjustments around life/schedule 
Less of:

* Overly complicated sessions 
* Too many optional extras 
* Big spikes in load 
* Punishing missed sessions 
Session bias: Simple, repeatable, sustainable.
What the app should not do: Do not overload the athlete just because they are motivated for one week.
Simple goal rules
Make the senior team = balanced physical standards and consistency.
Dominate your level = slightly more performance bias.
Feel fresh on game day = freshness and recovery bias.
Stay injury-free = availability and gradual load bias.
Get stronger and fitter = balanced strength + conditioning.
Build muscle = more hypertrophy where phase allows.
Stay consistent = simple and sustainable.
Main rule:
Goals should change the emphasis by about 5-15%, not create a completely different program.
This keeps it simple and stops goals from making the programming messy.
13. Progression rules - build the diary into this
Main idea
LFA progression should be simple. The app does not need to force exact weekly progressions for every lift or conditioning session.
The athlete should log what they did, then slowly try to improve over time through:
more weight
more reps
better quality
more distance
faster time
more calories
same work feeling easier
Progression should happen when the athlete is handling the work well, not just because the calendar says it is time to progress.
How to progress strength
Strength progression should be based on the athlete’s logged weights and how the session felt.
If the athlete completes the work well and feels good:
Add a small amount of weight next time.
If the athlete completes the work but it feels hard:
Repeat the same weight next time.
If the athlete misses reps, feels cooked, or form is poor:
Lower the weight slightly or reduce volume.
Simple rule:
Good session = small nudge up.
Average session = repeat.
Bad session = hold or back off.
In-season progression should be slower. The goal is mainly to maintain strength and stay fresh.
How to progress accessories
Accessories do not need strict progression.
They should be done with good control, good intent, and a weight that feels useful.
Progression can happen naturally through:
slightly heavier weight
more reps
better control
better pump
better tolerance
But the app should not obsess over progressing every accessory.
Simple rule:
Main lifts are tracked properly.
Accessories are useful support work.
How to progress conditioning
Conditioning progression should be based on the type of session.
For trackable conditioning sessions, the athlete should be able to log:
time
distance
pace
calories
rounds completed
intervals completed
machine used
RPE / how hard it felt
Examples that should be tracked properly:
1km repeats
MAS intervals
4x4s
calorie EMOMs
time trials
bike intervals
rower intervals
ski intervals
assault bike efforts
Examples that do not need heavy tracking:
easy flush
recovery walk
light bike
mobility/recovery circuit
basic sprint exposure
Progression can be:
same work faster
same time more distance
same interval more calories
same session lower RPE
more reps/rounds
slightly less rest
longer duration
The app should not progress conditioning aggressively if the athlete is in-season, sore, sick, injured, or already getting a lot of team training/game load.
How to progress sprint exposure
Sprint work should not be progressed like conditioning.
The goal is quality exposure, not fatigue.
Progression can be:
slightly more reps
slightly longer distance
better quality
more consistent exposure
But sprint work should stay low volume and high quality.
Do not chase fatigue in sprint sessions.
When to deload
Deload or back off when:
athlete is cooked
sleep is poor
soreness is high
performance drops
injury/niggle appears
conditioning feels brutal repeatedly
multiple sessions are missed
game load/team training load is high
A deload can mean:
less volume
lighter loads
fewer hard sessions
more recovery
repeat same week
remove finishers
When to repeat a week
Repeat a week when:
the athlete missed key sessions
the athlete did not complete enough work
the week was disrupted by illness/busy schedule
the athlete struggled with the current dose
the athlete needs more time before progressing
Do not progress just because a week passed.
When to back off
Back off when:
pain increases
readiness is poor
athlete is sick
athlete is very sore
hard sessions feel worse than expected
game day is close
team training load is high
What feedback changes next week
Feedback should influence future programming.
If the athlete logs:
felt good
completed everything
not too sore
The app can gently progress.
If they log:
too hard
missed reps
very sore
cooked
could not complete
The app should hold or reduce the next similar session.
What missed sessions do
One missed session should not ruin the program.
If one session is missed:
move it forward if it still fits
skip it if the week is already full
do not cram it into a bad spot
If multiple sessions are missed:
reduce expectations
restart momentum
do not force catch-up overload
What “too easy” does
If one session is too easy:
small progression next time
If several similar sessions are too easy:
increase the dose properly
Examples:
slightly heavier lift
more reps
more conditioning intervals
faster pace
more calories
slightly less rest
What “too hard” does
If one session is too hard:
repeat or slightly reduce next time
If several similar sessions are too hard:
reduce the program dose
Examples:
less weight
fewer sets
less conditioning volume
more rest
off-feet conditioning
easier session type
14. Program modification rules
This section is for the tap system. The goal is simple:
User taps what they want.
The app changes the program.
Coach Notes explain anything ongoing.
The user can clear or update it later.
Today only
What it means: The change only affects the selected day/session. It does not change future weeks or become a long-term rule.
Examples:

* Remove Bench Press from today’s session. 
* Swap today’s Rower flush to Bike. 
* Move today’s session to tomorrow. 
* Skip today’s conditioning. 
* Add core to today’s session. 
Should it create Coach Note? Usually no.
A Coach Note is only needed if the reason is still actively affecting the program.
Example:
Remove Bench Press today only because you're short on time = no Coach Note.
Avoid Bench Press because shoulder hurts and future weeks too = Coach Note.
Future weeks too
What it means: The user wants this change to affect similar sessions going forward.
This should not blindly copy today’s exact workout into every future week. It should create a rule or preference that the app uses intelligently.
Examples:

* Avoid Bench Press in future upper sessions. 
* Replace Rower with Bike where possible. 
* Add more core work to upper sessions. 
* Avoid heavy hinging while hamstring issue is active. 
* Use off-feet conditioning while calf/Achilles is sore. 
Should it create Coach Note? Yes.
If it affects future programming, the athlete should be able to see it in Coach Notes and clear it.
Example Coach Note:
Bench Press adjustment active
Similar sessions will avoid or replace Bench Press.
[ Clear adjustment ] [ Update ]
Ongoing modifier
What it means: An ongoing modifier is anything currently changing the program away from normal.
It can come from injury, fatigue, sickness, equipment, schedule, missed sessions, or user preferences.
Examples:

* Recovery mode active. 
* Groin issue active. 
* Rower adjustment active. 
* Busy week active. 
* Away days active. 
* Training paused for injury. 
* No heavy hinge while hamstring is active. 
How to clear: The user should be able to clear it through Coach Notes, not by typing to the AI coach.
Examples:
I'm good now
I'm all good now
I've been cleared
Clear adjustment
Back to normal
Clearing the modifier should stop it affecting the program and update/rebuild the week if needed.
Session swap
Rules: Session swap means changing the type of session on a day.
Examples:
Lower Strength -> Upper Strength
Hard Conditioning -> Recovery
Conditioning -> Rest
Upper Push -> Upper Pull
Rules:

* Keep game day and team training protected. 
* Do not create a bad hard-session stack. 
* Warn the user if they are adding too much. 
* Prefer a suitable session based on phase, game proximity, team training, injuries and current week load. 
* If user overrides the warning, allow it where safe. 
* If the swap is only for today, no ongoing Coach Note is needed. 
* If the swap creates an ongoing rule, create a Coach Note. 
Add to day
Rules: Add to day means stacking something extra onto an existing day.
Examples:
Add easy conditioning after upper body.
Add core to a strength session.
Add recovery work after team training.
Add a short pump/prehab session.
Rules:

* The app should check total daily and weekly load. 
* Adding easy/recovery work is usually okay. 
* Adding hard conditioning or lower strength should trigger a warning if the week is already heavy. 
* Do not hide hard work inside a day and pretend it is easy. 
* Do not add conditioning to pre-season team training days unless there is a clear reason. 
* If user adds too much, warn but still let them override where safe. 
* Today-only add = usually no Coach Note. 
* Future weeks too = create a Coach Note/modifier. 
Move session
Rules: Move session means taking a session from one day and putting it on another.
Rules:

* Do not lose the session. 
* Do not duplicate the session. 
* Check the destination day for game/team training conflicts. 
* Warn if it creates a bad stack. 
* Moving around game day should protect freshness. 
* Moving a lower-body or hard conditioning session close to game day should warn the user. 
* If moving to a day with another session, treat it as a double day and check total load. 
* One-off move usually does not need a Coach Note. 
Example:
Move Lower Strength from Monday to Wednesday.
If Wednesday is close to game day, warn:
"This is close to game day and may affect freshness."
Bin session
Rules: Bin session means removing a session.
Examples:
Remove today's gym session.
Remove conditioning.
Remove team training from this week only.
Remove the whole day.
Rules:

* Confirm before removing. 
* If there are multiple sessions on the day, ask which one to remove. 
* Do not accidentally remove team training or game day anchors unless user clearly chooses it. 
* If team training is removed for one date, do not permanently change normal team training days. 
* Today-only bin = usually no Coach Note. 
* If binning creates an ongoing restriction or preference, create a Coach Note. 
Example:
Can't make team training tonight
= remove this date only
= normal team training remains next week
Exercise swap
Rules: Exercise swap happens inside a session.
Examples:
Bench Press -> DB Floor Press
Rower -> Bike
Pull-ups -> Lat Pulldown
RDL -> Hip Thrust
Rules:

* First try to keep the same movement pattern. 
* If that is not tolerated, train the same or similar muscle group. 
* If that is not tolerated, train an unaffected area. 
* If that is not appropriate, use recovery or easy conditioning. 
* Do not swap to basically the same aggravating thing. 
Good swap:
Bench hurts -> neutral-grip DB Floor Press
Bad swap:
Bench hurts -> lighter Bench Press
Today only:

* change only today’s session 
* no Coach Note unless still active 
Future weeks too:

* create a modifier 
* show Coach Note 
* affect similar future sessions 
Injury modifier
Rules: An injury modifier changes the program around a body area, severity and triggers.
Examples:
Groin issue triggered by sprinting/COD.
Hamstring issue triggered by RDLs/sprinting.
Shoulder issue triggered by pressing.
Lower back issue triggered by hinging/rowing.
Rules:

* LFA does not diagnose or rehab. 
* Avoid the obvious aggravator. 
* Reduce load, volume, speed, range or intensity depending on severity. 
* Keep safe work in where possible. 
* Anything 6/10 or above should recommend physio/medical advice. 
* 8-10/10 pauses affected training. 
* Injury modifiers should always create Coach Notes. 
* The user should clear/update the injury through tap controls, not chat. 
Example Coach Note:
Hamstring issue active
Your program is being adjusted around this issue.
[ I'm all good now ] [ Update injury ]
Busy week / away
Rules: Busy week or away means the athlete’s normal schedule is disrupted.
Examples:
Busy week at work.
Away for holiday.
Cannot train Tuesday/Wednesday.
Only have time for two short sessions.
Rules:

* Reduce the week to the most important work. 
* Keep game/team training anchors where appropriate. 
* Remove optional extras first. 
* Remove hard conditioning before key strength/game preparation if needed. 
* Do not cram missed work into bad spots. 
* If away days are selected, clear/reduce those days. 
* Create a Coach Note while the busy/away adjustment is active. 
* Clearing the Coach Note should restore/update the week where possible. 
Example Coach Note:
Busy week active
Your week has been reduced around your schedule.
[ Back to normal ] [ Update ]
Simple program modification rules

* Today only = change this session/day only. 
* Future weeks too = create a rule for similar future sessions. 
* Ongoing modifier = anything currently changing the program. 
* Coach Notes must show every ongoing modifier. 
* Clearing the Coach Note must stop the modifier affecting the program. 
* The app should warn when the user is adding too much. 
* The user can override warnings where safe. 
* Game day and team training are anchors. 
* Do not send routine changes to AI chat. 
* Use AI chat only when the app genuinely cannot resolve the change safely.
15. What AI is allowed to do
Main idea
AI can help LFA feel smart, but it should not be the final authority on the program.
The important S&C rules should live in deterministic code. That means the app has fixed rules for game day, team training, injuries, fatigue, weekly load, sets/reps ranges, session placement, Coach Notes and tap-based changes.
AI can help inside those rules, but it should not be allowed to ignore them.
AI can

* Help choose between safe exercise options. 
* Suggest a similar exercise when the user has no equipment or pain. 
* Help understand messy wording or unusual user input. 
* Write plain-English explanations for why the program changed. 
* Fill in details only if the deterministic rules have already decided what is allowed. 
* Help with edge cases when the tap system cannot confidently solve the issue. 
AI cannot

* Override game-day protection. 
* Ignore team training load. 
* Diagnose injuries. 
* Prescribe rehab. 
* Tell someone to train through a serious issue. 
* Ignore Coach Notes. 
* Ignore injury restrictions. 
* Invent random sets/reps. 
* Add unsafe progressions. 
* Say it changed the program when it didn’t. 
* Remove or change sessions without going through the app’s rules. 
Deterministic code must own

* Session placement. 
* Weekly structure. 
* Game day and team training anchors. 
* Hard exposure caps. 
* Strength sets/reps ranges. 
* Conditioning dose ranges. 
* Injury severity rules. 
* Fatigue/sickness/load-reduction rules. 
* Coach Notes. 
* Modifier clearing. 
* Tap-based program changes. 
* Program rebuild rules. 
* Final validation before anything changes. 
Simple rule
AI can suggest.
The app rules decide.
The app validates.
Then the program changes.
That’s the clean model. The AI can be the brainy assistant, but your deterministic rules are the coach’s standards.
16. “Never do this” rules
These are the hard guardrails. The app should avoid these unless the user clearly overrides and the app gives a warning.
Game day / in-season rules

* Do not smash legs close to game day. 
* Do not put heavy lower-body strength, hard conditioning, sprint work or hard COD too close to a game. 
* Do not chase soreness in-season. 
* Do not make in-season about building huge new strength, size, mobility or conditioning blocks. 
* Do not add random hard extras late in the week just because the athlete has an available day. 
* Do not treat bye week as an excuse to destroy the athlete. 
Load management rules

* Do not stack too many hard exposures in one week. 
* Do not stack hard lower strength, hard conditioning, sprint/COD and team training without a very good reason. 
* Do not treat team training as recovery. 
* Do not ignore team training when calculating weekly load. 
* Do not punish athletes for missing a session by cramming everything back in. 
* Do not make up missed work if it creates a worse week. 
* Do not keep progressing when the athlete is clearly cooked, sick, sore or sleeping badly. 
Injury rules

* Do not diagnose injuries. 
* Do not prescribe rehab. 
* Do not tell an athlete to train through severe pain. 
* Do not give hard sprinting or COD to active groin, hamstring, calf, Achilles, knee or ankle issues. 
* Do not prescribe heavy hinge work for bad hamstring or lower-back issues. 
* Do not prescribe painful pressing for shoulder issues. 
* Do not swap an aggravating exercise for almost the same aggravating exercise. 
* Do not ignore injury triggers the athlete has already told the app. 
* Do not hide injury restrictions. If it affects the program, it must show in Coach Notes. 
* Do not keep affected work in the program when the athlete rates the issue 8-10 / 10. 
Conditioning rules

* Do not make every conditioning session hard. 
* Do not turn speed work into conditioning. 
* Do not add hard conditioning near game day when the athlete already has team training and games. 
* Do not add running volume when lower-limb pain is active. 
* Do not use hard running when off-feet conditioning would solve the problem. 
* Do not keep increasing conditioning if the athlete repeatedly says it feels brutal. 
Strength rules

* Do not use random exercises in-season that the athlete has not built tolerance to. 
* Do not chase high-rep lower-body soreness in-season. 
* Do not max out or grind main lifts in-season. 
* Do not add volume just because someone is advanced. 
* Do not change exercises so often that the athlete never gets better at the basics. 
* Do not make accessories more important than the main patterns. 
* Do not let AI invent random sets and reps outside the rules. 
App / AI rules

* Do not send routine actions to chat. 
* Do not let AI override game day, team training, injury restrictions or hard exposure rules. 
* Do not let AI say it changed the program unless the program actually changed. 
* Do not keep hidden modifiers active without Coach Notes. 
* Do not create Coach Notes for normal setup values like name, position, normal game day or normal team training. 
* Do not let a tap action collect information and then do nothing with it. 
* Do not let a program change happen without either being one-off obvious or creating a visible modifier. 
* Do not make the athlete explain simple things in free text when taps can solve it. 
Simple rule
Do not make the program harder, riskier, noisier or more confusing than it needs to be.
Protect freshness.
Respect team training and game day.
Train around issues.
Keep useful work in.
Explain active restrictions.
Let the athlete override where safe.
17. Addendum: definitions, conflict rules and final programming hierarchy
This addendum exists to clarify the whole LFA Programming Rules document.
If an earlier section is unclear or slightly conflicts with this addendum, use this addendum as the final rule.
A. Key definitions
Core session
A core session is a session the app is actively trying to include because it matters for the week.
Examples:
Lower strength
Upper strength
Full body strength
Hard conditioning
Team training
Game day
Important recovery after game
Core sessions should be protected where possible, but can still be reduced, moved, skipped or changed when life, injury, soreness, sickness, travel or game-day freshness require it.
Optional session
An optional session is useful, but not essential.
Examples:
Gunshow
Extra accessories
Mobility flow
Easy flush
Extra aerobic
Extra arms
Extra prehab
Optional sessions should be easy to skip without making the athlete feel like they failed.
Hard exposure
A hard exposure is one stressful training/event unit.
Examples:
Game
Hard team training
Lower strength
Full body strength
Sprint/COD
Hard conditioning
Repeated sprint
High-volume running
Heavy hinge/squat
A hard exposure can happen on its own or as part of a double day.
Example:
Tuesday upper strength + team training = 2 exposures
Hard day
A hard day is a calendar day that contains one or more hard exposures.
Example:
Tuesday upper strength + team training = 1 hard day, 2 exposures
This matters because LFA often prefers stacking compatible work on the same day to create more full rest days.
High / medium / low stress
Use these buckets when deciding how stressful a session is.
High stress:
Game
Hard team training
Lower strength
Hard full body
Heavy hinge/squat
Sprint/COD
Hard conditioning
Repeated sprint
High-volume running
Medium stress:
Upper strength
Moderate full body
Tempo conditioning
Moderate aerobic
Moderate accessories Power work classification
Low-volume power work can be medium stress when it is short, crisp and low volume.
Examples:
- 2-3 x 3 vertical jumps
- 2-3 x 3 speed bench
- 2-3 x 3 explosive push-ups
Power work becomes high stress when it is high impact, high volume, close to max effort, paired with hard lower-body work, or placed near sprint/COD/team/game load.
Examples:
- high-volume box jumps
- repeated broad jumps
- RFE split squat jumps
- hard jump work close to game day
- power work with active knee/calf/Achilles/groin/hamstring issues
Low stress:
Recovery
Mobility
Walk
Easy bike flush
Upper pump
Gunshow
Easy core
Light prehab
Important: a session can move up or down depending on the athlete.
Example:
Upper strength for a beginner may be high stress.
Upper strength for an advanced athlete may be medium stress.
Tempo for a poor runner may be high stress.
Tempo for a very fit athlete may be medium stress.
B. Weekly caps and default limits
These are default app-programmed limits. The user can add more where safe, but the app should warn.
Strength
The app should not normally program more than:
4 main strength sessions per week
This does not include:
Gunshow
Accessories
Mobility
Recovery
Light prehab
If the user tries to add a 5th main strength session, warn.
Conditioning
The app can program up to:
3-5 conditioning exposures per week
This depends on phase, team training, game day, fitness level, injury status and readiness.
In-season, team training and games count as conditioning load.
Default easy-aerobic modality weighting
When there is no explicit athlete modality preference and the app is choosing a default modality for easy aerobic, aerobic_base or zone-2 work, use a deterministic weighted selection:

* Bike: 40%
* Mixed erg block: 40%
* RowErg: 10%
* SkiErg: 10%

Bike and mixed erg work are the preferred defaults for steady aerobic work when the required equipment is available. RowErg and SkiErg remain valid rotation options, but should not dominate long easy-aerobic defaults.
A mixed erg block may include bike plus RowErg or SkiErg. No continuous RowErg or SkiErg block should be longer than 10 minutes. Longer RowErg or SkiErg aerobic work must be intervalised, with complete rest between aerobic blocks rather than easy work during the rest period.
This weighting is a deterministic fallback, not runtime randomness. A specific programmed modality, an explicit athlete preference and equipment availability override the default weighting.
Running exposure
Running exposure includes:
Game
Team training
Sprint session
COD session
Repeated sprint running
Tempo run
Hard running intervals
Long run
Running exposure does not include:
Bike
Ski
Rower
Assault bike
Swimming - do not program this in but an athlete may choose to add swimming in
Walking
Mobility
Default cap:
No more than 4 running exposures per week programmed by the app.
The user can override and add more running, but the app should warn.
Sprint/COD exposure
Team training and games usually count as sprint/COD exposure.
Default cap:
2-3 sprint/COD exposures per week max.
In-season, if the athlete has 2 team trainings and a game, the app usually does not need to add sprint/COD.
C. Game-day proximity rules
G-1
G-1 means the day before game day.
Default rule:
No heavy lifting.
No hard conditioning.
No hard lower.
No sprint/COD.
Acceptable options:
Rest
Recovery
Gunshow
Light accessories
Mobility
Very easy flush if wanted
G-2
G-2 means two days before game day.
Default rule:
No full hard lower-body session.
No extra hard sprint/COD.
No hard conditioning unless it is normal team training.
Team training often happens on G-2. That is accepted as normal club load.
A tiny low-volume neural/power exposure may be okay for experienced athletes, but it is not a proper lower session.
Example:
2 x 3 high box squat
2 x 3 vertical jumps
Very low volume
No grinding
No soreness chasing
This should be rare and only if the athlete is used to it.
G+1
G+1 means the day after game day.
Default rule:
Rest or recovery.
The user can choose an easy flush if they like moving after games, but the app should not force hard work.
Avoid:
Hard conditioning
Heavy lower
Sprint/COD
Big catch-up session
D. Double-day rules
LFA can use double days. Double days are often better than spreading hard work across every day.
Good double-day pairings:
Upper strength + team training
Upper strength + conditioning
Lower strength + easy off-feet conditioning
Full body + easy aerobic
Gunshow + easy flush
Strength + mobility
Risky or bad pairings:
Heavy hinge + sprint session
Heavy lower + hard running intervals
Hard COD + heavy lower
Hard conditioning + hard team training
Lower strength + upper strength as two full sessions
If a day would become two separate full gym sessions, prefer a single full-body session instead.
E. Team training rules
Team training is never recovery.
Team training should count as:
Running load
Sprint exposure
COD exposure
Skills
Contact/load
General fatigue
Conditioning exposure
Use this simple scale:
Light team training = skills/touch, low running
Moderate team training = normal training
Hard team training = lots of running, sprinting, contact or match sim
If the athlete does not say otherwise, assume team training is moderate to hard.
If team training is hard, reduce extra running, sprinting and hard conditioning around it.
F. Warning levels and override rules
There are three levels:
Soft warning
Strong warning
Hard stop
Soft warning
Use when the athlete is adding more than ideal, but it is not clearly unsafe.
Examples:
Extra easy aerobic
Extra upper body
Extra gunshow
Extra accessory work
5th conditioning exposure
Message style:
You can add this, but your week is already fairly full. Keep it easy if you are feeling flat.
Strong warning
Use when the athlete is likely affecting freshness, recovery or injury risk.
Examples:
Extra hard conditioning near game day
Heavy lower close to game day
Adding hard work after a game
Adding sprint/COD when already loaded
5th main strength session

Team training credit

* A normal unrestricted full TT session counts as 1 core conditioning exposure.
* It counts as 1 sprint/high-speed exposure only when normal high-speed running is expected and completed.
* Modified, rehab, restricted, non-contact or reduced-running participation does not automatically receive sprint credit.
* A generic hard-training label is not proof of high-speed work.
* Team-training anchor status never counts as main strength. A strength exposure on a TT day counts only when a deliberate app-authored main-strength contribution is separately planned and remains visible in the final workout.

Game and practice-match credit

* Normal participation counts as 1 core conditioning exposure, 1 sprint/high-speed exposure and 1 hard day.
* Reduced participation requires an explicit adjustment.
* A game or practice-match anchor never counts as main strength.

App top-ups

* Add only the remaining requirement after genuine anchor credit.
* Do not add unnecessary sprint after the floor is met.
* Optional light flushouts never count as core conditioning.
* A genuine sprint exposure requires actual high-speed running, not only skills, tempo work or low-speed COD.

Team training and games are fixed anchors. Declared availability governs additional app sessions, subject to the fixture-conditioned target-week rule below; it does not remove real-world anchors.

Fixture-conditioned availability

* A scheduled game or practice match occupies its fixture day; that occupation is not permanent athlete unavailability.
* Removing a fixture releases its former day for app training by default. Provenance is `released_game_day` or `released_practice_match_day`.
* A known in-season bye conditionally releases the athlete's usual game day with provenance `bye_usual_game_day`.
* Moving a fixture releases the old day and occupies the new day in one atomic target-week resolution.
* Effective target-week app availability is: explicit athlete availability, plus released fixture days, minus current fixture days, explicit unavailable/rest/commitment days, and safety/readiness restrictions.
* A released fixture day remains unavailable when an explicit unavailable-day constraint, calendar rest/commitment mark, active injury/readiness restriction, full pause, or another fixture blocks it.
* Stored `preferredTrainingDays` and `trainingDaysPerWeek` are not mutated by fixture resolution. Effective weekly training capacity is derived for the target week only.
* Removing a fixture must not decrease effective app availability unless another explicit blocker applies. Adding a fixture must not increase it.
* Fixture release changes availability, not coaching mode: bye build may use the released usual game day for the approved hard replacement exposure, while bye recovery may use it for rest, recovery aerobic or lighter strength and must not automatically receive hard conditioning.

Workout names, CORE labels, stale `workoutType`, weekday assumptions, array position and temporary UI state are not deletion identity.

Required core relocation prefers an existing compatible hard day when safe, then another effective available day.

Reject only malformed removal identity or a genuine technical/atomic-publication failure.

Whole-week repair and derived-session lifecycle

* Field actions still contribute to fatigue, hard-day load and spacing decisions.
* When team or game-like load is high, reduce or remove app power primers.

D. Strength-pattern rules

* Default to push, pull, squat and hinge in every healthy pre-season, mid/late off-season and in-season week.
* If a pattern is removed earlier in the week, modify a later session to restore it where safe.
* Rolling two-week balance is only a fallback when game spacing, availability, readiness or injury genuinely prevents weekly coverage.
* Three-session weeks may use full-body sessions to cover several patterns.
* Injury policy must record both required safe patterns and prohibited affected patterns.
* Accessories and gunshow never count toward main-strength frequency or pattern requirements.
* A session receives credit for multiple patterns only when it contains a meaningful main-strength contribution to each credited pattern.
* Examples of valid multi-pattern credit include squat + press + row for squat/push/pull, squat + RDL for squat/hinge, and bench + pull-ups for push/pull.
* Token accessory work does not earn full pattern credit. Injury-prohibited patterns cannot receive credit.
* Across each healthy week, default to equal or near-equal numbers of meaningful main lifts for push, pull, squat and hinge.
* Full-body sessions must not repeatedly overrepresent one pattern while underdosing another. Three full-body sessions with several push main lifts but only one meaningful pull main lift across the week are not a valid default balance.
* Any intentional imbalance must be justified by season phase, athlete goal, injury, readiness, availability or another explicit constraint.

E. Readiness, injury, equipment and invalid weeks

Low/cooked readiness

* Remove power primers.
* Reduce volume and intensity first.
* Preserve frequency where safe.
* Reduce frequency only with a typed `low_readiness` reason.
* Keep real-world anchors but reduce extra app work.
* In-season, preserve 2 strength exposures only when safe.

Injury

* Remove prohibited affected patterns and sprint work.
* Continue unaffected strength and conditioning.
* TT receives conditioning or sprint credit only when the athlete can participate normally in the relevant work.
* Final generation must not restore prohibited work.

Full pause or red flag

* No training.
* Recovery-only guidance and advice not to train may remain visible, but cannot count as training.

Equipment

Substitute before reducing frequency. Consider, as safe and appropriate:

* running;
* walking;
* bodyweight conditioning;
* hills;
* available ergs; and
* safe mixed conditioning.

Reduce frequency only when no safe substitute is feasible, using `equipment_infeasibility`.

Invalid-week handling

Do not store a week when:

* a required safe exposure cannot be placed and no approved typed reduction represents the unavoidable shortfall;
* prohibited injury work returns;
* a red flag remains unresolved; or
* the final week contradicts its approved reduction.

Repair or regenerate automatically. Use a safe fallback if repair fails. Athlete-requested deletion is the explicit exception to rejecting a required-exposure shortfall: preserve the deletion and apply the minimum `explicit_user_override` typed reduction after relocation, substitution and optional-work removal are exhausted. Show a simple settings-review message only when no safe week can be created. Red-flag/full-pause cases show recovery-only guidance and advice not to train.

F. Deload rules

* Pre-season automatically deloads every fourth week.
* The first four-week Off-season block is an explicit exception: Weeks 1–2 are early, Weeks 3–4 are mid, and Week 4 does not automatically deload.
* After the first block, late-off-season blocks may use the approved automatic fourth-week deload policy. A deload never resets the subphase.
* In-season deload occurs through bye recovery or a readiness/injury decision.
* Preserve strength and conditioning frequency where possible.
* Reduce sets, volume, duration, load and intensity before reducing frequency.
* Preserve 1 controlled sprint exposure unless explicitly reduced.
* Remove power primers.
* Pre-season retains strength minimum 3/default 4 and conditioning minimum 3/default 4.
* Off-season conditioning may reduce by 1 exposure when required.
* Keep off-season strength frequency stable where possible.

Hard-day policy

* Default weekly shape is 4 hard days plus 1 moderate day.
* 3 hard days is valid when phase, readiness, availability or injury requires it.
* 4 hard days is the preferred/default shape, not a universal blocking maximum.
* Contract v2 owns the permitted hard-day maximum for the selected phase and mode. Up to 5 hard days may be accepted in an in-season game or bye-build week, mid/late off-season build, pre-season build or practice-match week when fixture-relative spacing, participation, readiness, injury, exposure and full-rest rules all pass. Modes with a lower phase maximum retain that lower maximum.

* The final whole week is evaluated against the complete Section 18 contract. A count of 5 alone is advisory, not a blocking finding; unsafe G-relative placement, missing rest, prohibited work, invalid stacking or a phase-specific permitted-maximum breach remains blocking.
* Long slow aerobic work is medium stress when intensity is controlled.
* TT and games count as hard only with normal hard participation.
* A practice match counts as 1 hard day only with normal participation; reduced or modified participation requires explicit adjustment.
* Stack compatible stress rather than spreading hard work across every day.

| `injury_restriction` | Affected patterns, sprint or other unsafe work must be removed. |
| `equipment_infeasibility` | No safe substitution can preserve the exposure frequency. |
| `game_load_protection` | A game anchor requires a justified reduction to protect performance or safety. |
| `practice_match_load` | A practice-match anchor requires game-like spacing and dose adjustment. It does not authorise strength below 3 merely because the match exists: 2 TT + PM = S3; 1 TT + PM = S3; 0 TT + PM = S3–4, default 3. |
| `bye_recovery_mode` | The approved bye-recovery structure replaces the bye-build targets. |
| `deload_policy` | The approved deload rules change dose and, only where stated, frequency. |
| `spacing_safety_conflict` | Required work cannot be placed without violating game, sprint, lower-body or recovery spacing. |
| `training_age_limit` | The approved beginner structure requires reduced complexity, dose or frequency. |
| `full_pause` | Red-flag/full-pause policy removes all training. |
| `explicit_user_override` | The athlete explicitly chooses work outside the default after appropriate warning; it does not override a hard stop. |

An authorised reduction must identify the affected exposure domain and must survive into the final visible week. A final week that exceeds, restores or otherwise contradicts its approved reduction is invalid.

J. Phase-planner ownership and cross-path preservation

The Section 18 phase planner uses one ownership order for every generated week:

1. Resolve the canonical season phase, subphase and week mode.
2. Read that mode's required minimum, default target, preferred range and maximum from Section B.
3. Resolve typed target-week availability from stored athlete preferences, the prior and proposed fixture state, explicit calendar/unavailable marks and safety/readiness restrictions. Fixture release provenance and effective capacity are inputs to the contract; stored preferences remain unchanged.
4. Apply readiness, injury, participation, training-age and other applicable typed constraints. Any frequency change must carry an authorised reason from Section H.
5. Select the phase-owned strength, core-conditioning and sprint/high-speed targets before assigning weekdays.
6. Credit only qualifying team-training, game and practice-match anchors.
7. Allocate required main strength, `required_core` conditioning and required sprint/high-speed work.
8. Allocate any remaining `planner_selected_core` conditioning needed by the selected phase target.
9. Add `optional_flush`, `optional_recovery_aerobic`, gunshow and accessory work only after core work is satisfied. Optional work never rewrites the selected target or repairs a core shortfall.
10. Validate the allocation against the selected contract, preserve typed ownership through canonical workout construction, and validate the final effective week. A deficient week must repair, regenerate, safely reduce with a typed reason or reject; the target must never be rewritten to match deficient output.

This order applies to in-season game weeks, bye build, bye recovery, early/mid/late off-season, early/mid/late pre-season, practice-match weeks, deloads and every readiness, injury, availability, participation, training-age and equipment-constrained path. The phase table in Section B remains the source of the final target; weekday geometry only decides safe placement.

Cross-path rules

* Initial generation, edge-authored Week 1, deterministic fallback, rebuild, rollover and persistence/rehydration must preserve the same phase-owned contract and conditioning ownership.
* Generation, calendar mutation, rebuild, Repeat Week, rollover and persistence/rehydration must consume the same typed target-week availability resolver. No path may independently infer that a removed fixture day remains unavailable.
* A game or practice-match add, removal or move first attempts a minimal accepted-state repair: preserve every unaffected valid core session, anchor, `planEntryId`, weekday and prescription; remove fixture-only content; calculate the exact exposure delta; change optional work before core work; and add/move/broaden only the minimum sessions needed on effective available days.
* Among equally safe candidates meeting the same contract, choose deterministically by: Section 18 compliance, effective availability, preserved core sessions, changed-day count, preserved identities/prescriptions, use of a released fixture day, pattern balance, rest distribution, duplicate-pattern avoidance, avoidance of unnecessary consecutive active days, then optional-before-core sacrifice.
* Full regeneration is permitted only when no safe minimal-diff candidate can satisfy the target contract and final gateway.
* Rebuild, Repeat Week, rollover, persistence/rehydration, fixture changes and date/session changes use the same accepted-effective-week rebasing owner. They may project different target contracts, but they may not choose different mutation-source precedence.
* Home-card, workout-detail and Coach/chat deletion are producers of the same persisted athlete-removal constraint and accepted-state transaction. They must converge on the same accepted visible state for the same target and scope.
* Rebuild, Repeat Week and rollover may carry a user-removal constraint only onto its original concrete target date unless the athlete explicitly requests a broader recurring removal. They may not copy the prohibited work back onto that date, and rehydration must apply removal ownership before visible-ledger validation.
* Sunday fixtures protect the following Monday as G+1 across the week boundary. Monday in the same Monday-to-Sunday target week is G-6 and remains eligible when otherwise safe; it must not be reclassified as that fixture's G+1.
* Repeat Week uses the target week's canonical phase and selected exposure table. Its phase-table signature is the mode plus the selected main-strength, core-conditioning and sprint/high-speed targets.
* When source and target phase-table signatures differ, Repeat Week regenerates from the target phase table instead of copying the source target or deficient source structure.
* When the signatures match, source prescriptions may be preserved, but target anchors, typed constraints and final Section 18 validation still win.
* Detailed `required_core` and `planner_selected_core` identity remains attached to planner allocations and canonical workouts. An aggregate `core` compatibility projection may be used only by an unchanged legacy safety consumer; it does not replace the detailed phase-planner identity or allow optional work to receive core credit.
